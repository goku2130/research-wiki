# Scaling Laws for Reward Model Overoptimization

### Core Problem
In Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), a learned proxy reward model (RM) is used to optimize a policy. However, because the proxy is an imperfect representation of human preferences, excessive optimization against it can lead to "overoptimization"—a manifestation of Goodhart’s Law where the proxy score increases while the ground-truth (gold) reward decreases. The authors aim to quantify this effect and identify scaling laws that predict the gold reward as a function of optimization intensity.

### Method
To avoid the expense of human labeling, the authors employ a synthetic setup:
1.  **Gold Standard:** A fixed 6B parameter reward model serves as the "gold" ground truth.
2.  **Proxy Training:** Proxy RMs (ranging from 3M to 3B parameters) are trained on 100,000 synthetic comparisons generated by the gold RM.
3.  **Initial Policy:** GPT-3 series models are used as initial checkpoints, fine-tuned via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on InstructGPT demonstrations.
4.  **Optimization:** The authors optimize the policy against the proxy RM using two methods:
    *   **Best-of-$n$ (BoN) Sampling:** Generating $n$ trajectories and selecting the one with the highest proxy score.
    *   **Reinforcement Learning (RL):** Using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO).
5.  **Measurement:** The gold reward $R$ is measured against the distance $d$, defined as the square root of the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence from the initial policy: $d = \sqrt{D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi \parallel \pi_{\mathrm{init}})}$.
6.  **Recalibration:** RM scores are recentered so the initial policy average is 0, and gold RM variance is unit-normalized.

### Key Formulas
The authors find that the gold reward $R$ follows distinct functional forms based on the optimization method:

**Best-of-$n$ Sampling:**

$$
R_{\mathrm{bon}}(d) = d(\alpha_{\mathrm{bon}} - \beta_{\mathrm{bon}}d)
$$

**Reinforcement Learning:**

$$
R_{\mathrm{RL}}(d) = d(\alpha_{\mathrm{RL}} - \beta_{\mathrm{RL}}\log d)
$$

For BoN, the KL divergence is computed analytically as:

$$
\mathrm{KL}_{\mathrm{bon}} = \log n - \frac{n-1}{n}
$$

### Key Quantitative Results
*   **RM Parameter Scaling:** The coefficients $\alpha$ and $\beta$ scale smoothly with the number of proxy RM parameters, following approximate logarithmic trends. This allows for the prediction of peak gold RM scores based on RM size.
*   **RM Data Scaling:** A critical threshold of approximately 2,000 comparisons is required before the RM shows significant improvement over near-chance loss. Beyond this, more data reduces "goodharting" (overoptimization).
*   **Policy Size Independence:** Comparing 1.2B and 6B policies, the authors found that while larger policies start with higher gold rewards, they overoptimize at nearly the same KL distance and exhibit a similar gap between proxy and gold scores.
*   **RL vs. BoN:** RL is significantly less KL-efficient than BoN, consuming more KL to achieve similar levels of optimization. However, the relationship between proxy and gold scores remains similar across both methods.
*   **KL Penalty:** In RL, adding a KL penalty does not improve the gold RM score-KL frontier; it effectively acts as a form of early stopping.

### Stated Limitations
*   **Synthetic Gap:** The synthetic setup (using a 6B RM as a proxy for humans) may not perfectly transfer to real-world human preference settings.
*   **Intent Mismatch:** The study captures the mismatch between the proxy RM and gold labels, but not the mismatch between gold labels and actual human intent.
*   **Policy Scope:** Policy size scaling was only explored using two different model sizes.
*   **Adversarial Goodhart:** The models used were not powerful enough to implement active adversarial strategies; the authors warn that these scaling laws may break down if the system becomes capable of adversarial manipulation.
*   **Hyperparameters:** Results regarding the KL penalty may be sensitive to specific hyperparameter configurations.
