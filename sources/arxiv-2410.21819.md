# Summary: Self-Preference Bias in LLM-as-a-Judge

### Core Problem
The "LLM-as-a-judge" paradigm uses large language models (LLMs) to automate the evaluation of dialogue systems. However, these evaluators often exhibit **self-preference bias**, a tendency to overestimate the quality of their own generated outputs. This bias is problematic because it can inadvertently promote specific response styles or policies intrinsic to the evaluator rather than objective quality. Prior to this work, there were few reliable quantitative metrics to measure this bias or a clear understanding of its underlying causes.

### Method and Recipe
The authors propose a quantitative framework based on algorithmic fairness to measure self-preference bias using pairwise evaluations.

**1. Metric Development**
The authors adapt the concept of **Equal Opportunity** from fairness literature. In this context, the LLM evaluator is treated as a classifier. The bias is measured as the difference in the probability that the LLM prefers a response given that a human also preferred it, comparing cases where the response was self-generated versus generated by another model.

**2. Experimental Setup**
*   **Dataset:** The Chatbot Arena dataset, containing 33,000 dialogues (user query and a pair of responses) with human-labeled preferences.
*   **Evaluators:** Eight LLMs were tested: GPT-4, GPT-3.5-Turbo, Vicuna-7b, Vicuna-13b, oasst-pythia-12b, dolly-v2-12b, Koala-13b, and stablelm-tuned-alpha-7b.
*   **Procedure:**
    *   The evaluator is presented with a query and two responses (A and B).
    *   To mitigate **position bias**, the positions of responses A and B are swapped, and the resulting scores are averaged.
    *   To ensure interpretability across models with different probability distributions, the authors calculate a normalized score based on the output probabilities of the tokens "[[A]]" and "[[B]]".

**3. Causal Analysis (Perplexity Hypothesis)**
To investigate why LLMs prefer their own text, the authors hypothesized that the bias is driven by **perplexity**. They analyzed the relationship between the perplexity of the responses and the winning judgment rates of the LLMs compared to human evaluators.

### Key Formulas
The **self-preference bias** of an evaluator $f$ is defined as:

$$
\text{Bias} = P(Y' = 1 | S = 1, Y = 1) - P(Y' = 1 | S = 0, Y = 1)
$$

Where:
*   $Y \in \{0, 1\}$ is the index of the human-preferred response.
*   $Y' \in \{0, 1\}$ is the index of the $f$-preferred response.
*   $S \in \{0, 1\}$ is the index of the $f$-generated response.

The **normalized score** for response A is calculated as:

$$
\text{score}_{\mathrm{A}} = \frac{p(\mathsf{A}|\mathtt{context})}{\sum_{w\in\{\mathtt{A},\mathtt{B}\}}p(w|\mathtt{context})}
$$

For secondary analysis, the authors used a **Demographic Parity** bias metric:

$$
\text{Bias} = P(Y' = 1 | S = 1) - P(Y' = 1 | S = 0)
$$

### Key Quantitative Results
*   **Bias Magnitude:** GPT-4 exhibited a significant degree of self-preference bias. Using the Demographic Parity metric, GPT-4 had the highest bias score at **0.749**, followed by Vicuna-13b (**0.382**) and GPT-3.5-Turbo (**0.191**).
*   **Perplexity Correlation:** The analysis revealed that LLMs assign significantly higher evaluations to responses with lower perplexity than human evaluators do. This tendency was observed across most models (except dolly-v2-12b and stablelm-tuned-alpha-7b).
*   **Independence from Self-Generation:** The authors found that LLMs prefer low-perplexity text regardless of whether they generated it themselves. Because LLMs naturally produce text with lower perplexity relative to their own internal distribution, the "self-preference" is actually a "familiarity preference."
*   **Competitive Models:** Additional tests with Llama-2 and Llama-3 confirmed that even high-performing models exhibit a stronger correlation between low perplexity and high ratings than human evaluators.

### Stated Limitations
The authors noted that they were unable to obtain direct perplexity values for closed-source models (GPT-4 and GPT-3.5-Turbo), which necessitated the use of Llama models as proxies for the perplexity analysis. Additionally, they cautioned that the Demographic Parity metric does not account for intrinsic quality and should only be used to analyze how highly a model rates its own outputs, not as a definitive measure of "unjust" evaluation.
