The study addresses the supervision dilemma for language models generating step-by-step reasoning: whether to optimize exclusively for the final answer (outcome-based) or for each intermediate reasoning step (process-based). While outcome-based methods drastically reduce label requirements, they risk producing correct final answers alongside flawed reasoning traces, a critical issue in safety-critical and educational domains. The authors conduct a comprehensive comparison on the GSM8K math word problem dataset, evaluating trace error (incorrect steps despite a correct final answer) and final-answer error.

The methodology follows a structured pipeline. First, process-based supervised fine-tuning (SFT) maximizes the log-likelihood of human-provided reasoning traces using AdamW optimization (batch size 256) and early stopping at 70 steps. Second, reward models (RMs) are trained as stepwise binary classifiers. Outcome-supervised RMs (ORMs) predict final-answer correctness, while process-supervised RMs (PRMs) predict stepwise correctness using human annotations. Third, test-time decoding generates multiple samples (temperature 0.8) and selects the optimal output via RM-weighted decoding, formally defined as selecting the final answer $a^* = \arg\max_a \sum_{i: y_i=a} \text{RM}(y_i)$, where $y_i$ denotes candidate samples, followed by choosing the sample with the highest RM score among those yielding $a^*$. Fourth, reinforcement learning via expert iteration alternates policy improvement and distillation. Policy improvement employs three strategies: final-answer RL (filtering traces by correct answers), ORM-RL (selecting traces with the highest ORM score), and PRM-RL (stepwise beam search maximizing PRM scores up to 15 steps). Distillation then fine-tunes the policy on these expert samples over five epochs using SFT hyperparameters. Human annotation collects stepwise correctness labels by identifying the first major mistake in generated traces, yielding 9,856 binary labels across 1,560 samples after removing low-agreement annotators and ambiguous problems.

Quantitatively, the best-performing configuration (SFT combined with PRM-RL and PRM reranking) achieves a trace error of 3.8% and a final-answer error of 12.9%. The SFT+ORM-RL variant matches this with 3.4% trace error and 12.7% final-answer error, improving upon prior state-of-the-art final-answer error (16.8%) and trace error (14.0%). When the model is permitted to abstain on 30% of questions, the selective final-answer error drops to 2.7%. Notably, outcome-based and process-based SFT yield nearly identical final-answer errors without reward models (23.5% vs. 22.3%) and with ORMs (16.6% vs. 14.8%), demonstrating that outcome-based supervision requires only 1–4 tokens of label per question. Furthermore, ORMs trained solely on final answers approximate process-based labels with 85% agreement, surpassing their own 77% agreement with outcome labels. However, direct final-answer RL without process-emulating rewards yields high trace errors (12.4%), confirming that low trace error necessitates either explicit process feedback or a reward model that emulates it.

The study acknowledges several limitations. The high cost of recruiting domain-expert annotators restricts evaluation to the GSM8K dataset, preventing broader safety or alignment investigations. The finding that ORMs approximate PRM labels may be domain-specific, potentially relying on the mathematical structure of GSM8K where incorrect intermediate steps rarely yield correct final answers. Additionally, both majority voting and RM-weighted decoding depend on exact string matching for final answers, which limits their generalizability to tasks with non-deterministic or paraphrased outputs. Finally, the human-annotated dataset remains small (1,560 samples), and the RM training loss exhibits fluctuations, necessitating careful validation-based model selection.
