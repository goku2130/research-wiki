The study investigates the validity of the Weak Cosmic Censorship Conjecture (WCCC) for rotating black holes in semiclassical gravity governed by a type-A trace anomaly. Specifically, it tests whether dropping a test particle into an extremal or singular black hole can destroy the event horizon, thereby exposing a naked singularity and violating the WCCC.

The methodology proceeds through a systematic gedanken experiment. First, the authors utilize the analytic rotating black hole metric derived from semiclassical Einstein equations with a type-A anomaly, expressed in ingoing Kerr-like coordinates. The spacetime structure features a ring singularity at $\Sigma=0$ and a quantum-induced singularity at $r_s(\theta)$. The event horizon location $r_H(\theta)$ is determined by solving $[\partial_\theta r_H(\theta)]^2 + \Delta|_{r=r_H(\theta)} = 0$, where $\Delta = r^2 + a^2 - 2r\mathcal{M}(r, \theta)$. Second, the parameter space is partitioned into three distinct regions based on the coupling ratio $\alpha/M^2$: Region I ($-1 \le \alpha/M^2 \le 0$), Region II ($0 < \alpha/M^2 < 6.2754$), and Region III ($6.2754 \le \alpha/M^2 \le 8$). Third, a test particle of unit mass is introduced on the equatorial plane ($\theta=\pi/2$) with energy $E$ and angular momentum $L$, subject to the test-body assumption $E \ll M$ and $L \ll J$. Fourth, the capture condition for the particle to cross the horizon is established as $E > \Omega_H^{\text{equator}} L$, where $\Omega_H^{\text{equator}} = a/(a^2 + r_H^2)|_{\theta=\pi/2}$. Fifth, the black hole parameters undergo a first-order perturbation: $M \to M + \delta M$ and $J \to J + \delta J$, with $\delta M = E$ and $\delta J = L$. Sixth, the horizon-determining function $\Delta$ (or $\Delta_{\text{equator}}^{r_s}$ in Region III) is expanded to first order to derive the destruction condition. Finally, by comparing the destruction bound with the capture condition, the authors determine the allowable ratio $E/L$ required to overspin the black hole.

Key formulas governing the analysis include the trace anomaly relation $g^{\mu\nu} \langle T_{\mu\nu} \rangle = \frac{\beta}{2} C^2 - \frac{\alpha}{2} \mathcal{G}$ and the semiclassical Einstein equation $R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R = 8\pi G \langle T_{\mu\nu} \rangle$. The perturbation analysis relies on the linear expansion $\Delta' = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial M} \delta M + \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial J} \delta J$. The destruction thresholds are defined as $\Omega_{\text{I}} = - \frac{\partial_J \Delta_{\text{equator}}}{\partial_M \Delta_{\text{equator}}}$, $\Omega_{\text{II}} = - \frac{\partial_J \Delta_{\text{poles}}}{\partial_M \Delta_{\text{poles}}}$, and $\Omega_{\text{III}} = - \frac{\partial_J \Delta_{\text{equator}}^{r_s}}{\partial_M \Delta_{\text{equator}}^{r_s}}$. The violation criterion requires the interval $\Omega_H^{\text{equator}} < E/L < \Omega_{\text{region}}$ to be non-empty.

Quantitative results indicate that $\partial_M \Delta < 0$ holds across all parameter regions. Consequently, the allowed length $\Delta \Omega_{\text{region}} \equiv \Omega_{\text{region}} - \Omega_H^{\text{equator}}$ is strictly positive for the entire range of $\alpha/M^2$, except at three specific limits. The analysis demonstrates that WCCC is violated under first-order perturbation for all extremal and singular black holes in Regions I, II, and III, with the exception of the extremal Kerr limit ($\alpha/M^2 = 0$), the static extremal case ($\alpha/M^2 = -1$), and the static singular case ($\alpha/M^2 = 8$). For these exceptions, $\Delta \Omega = 0$, indicating resistance to overspinning at first order.

The authors explicitly acknowledge several limitations. The test particle dynamics are governed strictly by classical geodesic equations, entirely neglecting quantum corrections that may alter particle trajectories in the strong-curvature regime. Furthermore, the analysis is confined to first-order perturbation theory. The authors note that second-order effects and full dynamical backreaction must be considered to definitively confirm horizon destruction, particularly for near-extremal configurations. Future investigations are required to address second-order approximations and quantum-modified particle behaviors, especially for the three non-violating limits identified.
