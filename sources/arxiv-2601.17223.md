The source addresses a critical limitation in reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR): while outcome-level verification guarantees correct final answers, it provides no assurance that intermediate reasoning steps follow valid logic. Existing process supervision relies on neural judges, which reintroduce opacity, bias, and reward-hacking vulnerabilities. To resolve this, the authors propose Verifiable Process Reward Models (VPRMs), a framework that replaces learned judges with deterministic, rule-based verifiers to reward each intermediate step only when it satisfies explicit domain guidelines.

The VPRM methodology is instantiated on risk-of-bias (RoB) assessment in medical systematic reviews, a task governed by rigid Cochrane guidelines. The training pipeline proceeds as follows: (1) A corpus of 2,946 paper-risk pairs is constructed from CochraneForest and CochraneForestExt datasets. (2) Step-level annotations are synthetically generated using Llama 3.1 405B and human-verified. (3) During policy optimization, an input $x$ generates a reasoning trajectory $\tau$ via policy $\pi_\theta$. Each step $t$ outputs a step identifier $s_t$ and label $l_t$. (4) Correctness is evaluated by bounded scoring functions $V_{id}(s_t, s_t^*)$ and $V_{label}(l_t, l_t^*)$, which map model outputs and gold standards to $\{0, 1\}$. (5) The instantaneous step-level reward is computed as $r_t = w_{id} V_{id}(s_t, s_t^*) + w_{label} V_{label}(l_t, l_t^*)$. (6) A terminal outcome reward $r_{out}$ verifies if the final predicted risk matches the gold label. (7) The full verifiable process reward is $R(\tau) = \sum_{t=1}^T r_t + r_{out}$. (8) This reward signal is integrated into Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) or Dynamic sAmpling Policy Optimization (DAPO). GRPO normalizes rewards via $\hat{r}_i = \frac{r_i - \mu_r}{\sigma_r}$ and optimizes a clipped, KL-regularized objective, while DAPO removes the KL penalty, employs dynamic sampling to filter perfectly accurate or inaccurate prompts, and applies a token-level policy gradient loss.

Quantitative evaluations across CochraneForest, RoBBR Cochrane, and RoBBR Non-Cochrane datasets demonstrate VPRM’s efficacy. The Qwen2.5-7B-GRPO-VPRM model achieves 87.9% accuracy and 76.7% macro-F1 on CochraneForest, outperforming outcome-only RLVR (81.5% Acc, 70.2% F1) by 6.5% and surpassing state-of-the-art pretrained models by up to 20% in F1. On RoBBR Cochrane and Non-Cochrane, VPRM achieves 65.2%/58.5% F1 and 60.7%/47.2% F1, respectively, confirming generalization beyond the training distribution. VPRM consistently exceeds neural process-reward baselines, such as GPT-OSS judges (56.1% F1) and MedPRM (53.4% F1). A coherence metric, defined as $C(x) = \mathbb{I}(f(\{l_t\}) = y)$, confirms that VPRM-trained models produce reasoning traces internally consistent with their final predictions. Ablation studies reveal that removing the outcome reward causes substantial performance degradation, and verifying step structure alone is insufficient; the full VPRM requires both step-level correctness verification and outcome supervision.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. VPRMs are inherently constrained to domains that admit deterministic, rule-based verification and rigid decision paths, limiting immediate applicability to open-ended reasoning tasks. The framework’s theoretical guarantees rely on mild assumptions, including finite reward variance, strictly higher expected rewards for correct chains, and sufficiently large sampling groups. Furthermore, the neural PRM baselines used for comparison are approximated via LLM judges rather than domain-trained models, potentially underestimating neural process supervision capabilities. Finally, the synthetic step-level annotations depend on the quality and prompt design of the base LLM used for data generation, introducing a potential bottleneck in scalability.
