OrienterNet addresses the core problem of visual localization’s reliance on complex, expensive 3D point clouds that are computationally heavy and difficult to maintain at scale. Humans naturally navigate using compact 2D planimetric maps, yet machine learning systems have historically ignored this paradigm. The research bridges this gap by enabling sub-meter camera pose estimation using freely available 2D maps like OpenStreetMap (OSM), eliminating the need for costly 3D reconstruction, large storage footprints, and cloud infrastructure.

The method operates as a three-stage neural pipeline. First, a gravity-aligned query image $I$ with known pinhole calibration is processed by an image CNN $\Phi_{\text{image}}$ to extract features. These features are lifted to a neural Bird's-Eye View (BEV) $\mathbf{T}$ by predicting a distribution over log-distributed scales, which are interpolated to depth planes and resampled into a Cartesian grid alongside a confidence map $\mathbf{C}$. Second, OSM data (polygons, lines, points) centered around a coarse GPS prior $\xi_{\text{prior}}$ is rasterized and encoded by a map CNN $\Phi_{\text{map}}$ into a neural map $\mathbf{F}$ containing semantic features and a unary location prior $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$. Third, the system performs exhaustive template matching between $\mathbf{F}$ and $\mathbf{T}$ across a discretized 3-DoF pose space (2D position and $K$ rotations), generating a score volume $\mathbf{M}$. This is combined with $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ via softmax to produce a probability volume $\mathbf{P}$, from which the maximum likelihood pose is inferred. For sequences, individual probability volumes are warped to a reference frame and multiplied to resolve ambiguities.

Key mathematical formulations govern the cross-modal lifting and matching. The scale distribution is defined as $\boldsymbol{\sigma} = \left\{ \sigma_{\min} \left( \sigma_{\max} / \sigma_{\min} \right)^{i/S} \mid i \in \{0 \dots S\} \right\}$. Polar feature aggregation uses $\bar{\mathbf{X}}_{u,d} = \sum_v \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{u,d,v} \mathbf{X}_{u,v}$, where the scale-to-depth interpolation is $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{u,d,v} = \underset{v}{\text{softmax}} \left( \mathbf{S}_{u,v} \left[ f/d \cdot \Delta \right] \right)$. The final probability volume is computed as $\mathbf{P} = \text{softmax} \left( \mathbf{M} + \boldsymbol{\Omega} \right)$, with the matching score $\mathbf{M}[\boldsymbol{\xi}] = \frac{1}{UZ} \sum_{\mathbf{p} \in (U \times Z)} \mathbf{F}[\boldsymbol{\xi}(\mathbf{p})]^{\top} (\mathbf{T} \odot \mathbf{C}) [\mathbf{p}]$. Multi-view fusion follows $P (\boldsymbol {\xi} _ {i} | \{\mathbf {I} _ {j} \}, \text { map }) = \prod_ {k} P (\boldsymbol {\xi} _ {i} \oplus \hat {\boldsymbol {\xi}} _ {i j} | \mathbf {I} _ {j}, \text { map })$.

Quantitative evaluations demonstrate strong performance across domains. The model was trained on the Mapillary Geo-Localization (MGL) dataset, comprising 760,000 images from 12 cities. In driving scenarios evaluated on KITTI, OrienterNet achieved a position recall of 88.85% at 3m and 94.47% at 5m, with orientation recall of 73.51% at 3° and 89.45% at 5°. Fusing 20-second sequences pushed recall beyond 95% at 3m/3°. The architecture demonstrated strong zero-shot generalization, outperforming models trained directly on KITTI. In augmented reality tests using Aria glasses across Seattle and Detroit, multi-frame OrienterNet achieved 61.26% position recall at 3m in Seattle and 68.77% in Detroit, substantially surpassing consumer-grade GPS sensors, which peaked at 20.48% and 53.41% respectively. Inference requires 94 ms on an NVIDIA RTX 2080 GPU.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. The approach struggles in environments with few distinctive semantic elements or when map features are spatially inaccurate or missing, as OSM is crowd-sourced and inconsistently detailed across regions. Performance degrades proportionally with reduced field-of-view, and AR localization remains less accurate than driving scenarios due to challenging pedestrian viewpoints and noisy inertial sensors. Additionally, the system assumes a known gravity direction and a coarse GPS prior, which may be unreliable in extreme urban canyons.
