The paper addresses the unresolved debate over whether Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) fundamentally enhances Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning or merely improves sampling efficiency by reweighting pre-existing reasoning paths in the base model. Specifically, it investigates if RLVR extends the reasoning capability boundary and implicitly incentivizes correct Chain-of-Thought (CoT) generation despite rewards being derived solely from final answer correctness.

The authors systematically evaluate RLVR through a multi-stage empirical and theoretical pipeline. First, they introduce CoT-Pass@K, a metric requiring both correct final answers and valid intermediate reasoning steps. To compute this, they deploy an LLM-as-a-CoT-Judge paradigm using DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B as a verifier, applying three aggregation strategies across multiple independent verification attempts: any-correct, all-correct, and majority-correct. Second, they reproduce the GRPO-style RLVR training recipe (DAPO) starting from the Qwen2.5-32B base model on a curated set of mathematical problems. During training, they track per-prompt optimization metrics, including the fraction of correct answers $P(CA)^{(q)}$ and the fraction of correct CoTs within correct answers $P(CC|CA)^{(q)}$. Finally, they assess CoT quality by performing supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on checkpoints from different RLVR stages, using the generalization performance of the resulting SFT models as a proxy for reasoning quality.

The theoretical framework formalizes CoT and answer correctness indicators as $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i)$ and $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i)$. The verifiable reward is binary: $R(y_i)=\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i)$. The Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) advantage is computed as $\hat{A}(y_i) = \frac{R(y_i) - \mu_{\mathbf{Y}}}{\sigma_{\mathbf{Y}}}$, where $\mu_{\mathbf{Y}}$ and $\sigma_{\mathbf{Y}}$ denote the group mean and standard deviation of rewards. The policy gradient update follows $\nabla_{\theta} J(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \hat{A}(y_i) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_i \mid q)$. Under the Logic Prior assumption that correct CoTs induce correct answers with probability $\alpha$ and incorrect CoTs with probability $\beta$ (where $\alpha > \beta$), the authors prove that $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}(y_i) \mid \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i)=1] > 0$ and $\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}(y_i) \mid \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i)=0] < 0$, demonstrating that GRPO monotonically increases the probability of generating correct CoTs. Evaluation metrics are defined as $\text{Pass@K}^{(q)} = 1 - \frac{\binom{G-C}{K}}{\binom{G}{K}}$ and $\text{CoT-Pass@K}^{(q)} = 1 - \frac{\binom{G-D}{K}}{\binom{G}{K}}$, where $C$ and $D$ count answer passes and joint CoT-answer passes, respectively.

Empirical evaluations on AIME 2024 and AIME 2025 reveal that while standard Pass@K suggests the base model catches up to the RLVR-tuned model at large $K$, the CoT-Pass@K metric maintains a consistent, significant performance gap across all $K$ up to 1024, confirming an extended reasoning boundary. In competitive coding tasks on LiveCodeBench, post-RLVR models like AceReason-Nemotron-7B exhibit clear Pass@K improvements over distilled counterparts. Training dynamics show that $P(CA)^{(q)}$ approaches 1.0 after approximately 400 optimization steps for fully optimized training questions. However, the median $P(CC|CA)^{(q)}$ stabilizes around 0.7, indicating persistent flawed reasoning in roughly 30% of correct answers. Despite this, CoT-Pass@K on unseen AIME 2024 questions improves notably within the first 20 training steps. Furthermore, SFT on post-RLVR CoT data successfully replicates the Pass@1 performance of the fully RLVR-trained model, validating that RLVR generates fundamentally higher-quality reasoning trajectories.

The study acknowledges several constraints. The reliance on an LLM verifier for CoT correctness introduces potential false positives and negatives, necessitating costly manual inspection to ensure reliability. Theoretically, the derived theorem explains the optimization dynamics but offers no formal guarantee for generalization, which is only demonstrated empirically. Practically, the DAPO recipe exhibits a limitation where, once answer accuracy saturates ($P(CA)^{(q)} \to 1.0$), the GRPO advantage becomes invalid for uniformly correct groups, leaving a non-negligible portion of imperfect CoTs unmitigated by answer-only rewards. Additionally, the authors note that static benchmarks risk data contamination, emphasizing the need for live, challenging evaluation environments.
