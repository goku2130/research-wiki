This paper investigates the impact of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) on the reasoning abilities of Large Language Models (LLMs), particularly in the context of long chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning. The core problem addressed is the debate surrounding whether RLVR truly enhances LLM reasoning capabilities or merely improves sampling efficiency. Previous observations suggested that some post-RLVR models improve Pass@1 but not Pass@K, leading to a hypothesis that all correct reasoning paths are already present in the base model, and RLVR only adjusts sampling probabilities. This paper challenges that hypothesis.

**Method/Recipe Step by Step:**

1.  **Revisit Pass@K Experiments:** The authors re-evaluate the Pass@K metric for both mathematical and coding tasks, comparing base LLMs with post-RLVR models.
2.  **Introduce CoT-Pass@K Metric:** For mathematical tasks, where base LLMs might coincidentally arrive at correct answers with flawed reasoning, a new metric, CoT-Pass@K, is introduced. This metric requires both the final answer and the intermediate reasoning steps (CoT) to be correct for a successful evaluation.
3.  **LLM-as-a-CoT-Judge Paradigm:** To verify the correctness of mathematical CoTs at scale, DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B is employed as a verifier.
    *   **Multi-verification Approach:** For each CoT, multiple independent verifications (n=3) are performed.
    *   **Aggregation Strategies:** Three strategies are used to assess CoT correctness:
        *   **Any-correct:** At least one verification returns correct.
        *   **All-correct:** All verifications must return correct.
        *   **Majority-correct:** The majority vote determines the outcome.
    *   **Prompt Template for Verifier:** A specific prompt is used to instruct the verifier to analyze the solution step-by-step, identify issues (Calculation Error, Logical Error, Conceptual Error, Omission/Incompleteness, Other), and provide a final judgment (yes/no).
4.  **Theoretical Framework Development:** A theoretical explanation is provided for how RLVR, specifically the GRPO algorithm, incentivizes correct reasoning even when rewards are based solely on answer correctness.
    *   **Problem Setup:** Defines a question prompt `q`, `G` responses `Y`, CoT `c_i`, and final answer `a_i`.
    *   **Correctness Indicators:**
        *   $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i) = 1$ if $c_i$ is correct, 0 otherwise.
        *   $\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i) = 1$ if $a_i$ is correct, 0 otherwise.
    *   **Verifiable Reward:** $R(y_i) = \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i)$.
    *   **GRPO Advantage Calculation:** $\hat{A}(y_i) = \frac{R(y_i) - \mu_{\mathbf{Y}}}{\sigma_{\mathbf{Y}}}$, where $\mu_{\mathbf{Y}} = \frac{1}{G} \sum_{j=1}^{G} R(y_j)$ and $\sigma_{\mathbf{Y}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{G} \sum_{j=1}^{G} (R(y_j) - \mu_{\mathbf{Y}})^2}$.
    *   **Policy Gradient Update:** $\nabla_{\theta} J(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} \hat{A}(y_i) \nabla_{\theta} \log \pi_{\theta}(y_i \mid q)$.
    *   **Logic Prior Assumption:** $P(\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i) = 1 \mid \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i) = 1) = \alpha > P(\mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i) = 1 \mid \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i) = 0) = \beta$.
5.  **Analyze Training Dynamics:** The authors reproduce GRPO-style training using the DAPO recipe and analyze the evolution of key indicators.
    *   **Key Indicators:**
        *   Number of answer passes: $C = \sum_{i=1}^{G} \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i)$.
        *   Number of both CoT and answer passes: $D = \sum_{i=1}^{G} \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{CoT}}(c_i) \cdot \mathcal{I}_{\mathrm{Ans}}(a_i)$.
        *   Per-prompt Pass@K: $\text{Pass@K}^{(q)} = 1 - \frac{\binom{G-C}{K}}{\binom{G}{K}}$.
        *   Per-prompt CoT-Pass@K: $\text{CoT-Pass@K}^{(q)} = 1 - \frac{\binom{G-D}{K}}{\binom{G}{K}}$.
        *   Probability of correct answers: $P(CA)^{(q)} = \frac{C}{G}$.
        *   Probability of correct CoTs given correct answers: $P(CC|CA)^{(q)} = \frac{D}{C}$.
6.  **Evaluate CoT Quality via Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT):** SFT is performed on CoTs generated by models at different RLVR stages. The generalization performance (Pass@1) of these SFT models on test sets is used as a proxy for CoT quality.

**Key Formulas in LaTeX:**

*   **Correctness Indicators:**
    

$$
\mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{CoT}} (c _ {i}) = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} 1 & \text { if   } c _ {i} \text {   is   correct } \\ 0 & \text { otherwise } \end{array} \right., \quad \mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{Ans}} (a _ {i}) = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} 1 & \text { if   } a _ {i} \text {   is   correct } \\ 0 & \text { otherwise } \end{array} \right.
$$

*   **GRPO Advantage:**
    

$$
\hat {A} (y _ {i}) = \frac {R (y _ {i}) - \mu_ {\mathbf {Y}}}{\sigma_ {\mathbf {Y}}}, \quad \mu_ {\mathbf {Y}} = \frac {1}{G} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {G} R (y _ {j}), \quad \sigma_ {\mathbf {Y}} = \sqrt {\frac {1}{G} \sum_ {j = 1} ^ {G} (R (y _ {j}) - \mu_ {\mathbf {Y}}) ^ {2}}
$$

*   **Policy Gradient Update:**
    

$$
\nabla_ {\theta} J (\theta) \approx \frac {1}{G} \sum_ {i = 1} ^ {G} \hat {A} (y _ {i}) \nabla_ {\theta} \log \pi_ {\theta} (y _ {i} \mid q)
$$

*   **Logic Prior Assumption:**
    

$$
P (\mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{Ans}} (a _ {i}) = 1 \mid \mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{CoT}} (c _ {i}) = 1) = \alpha > P (\mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{Ans}} (a _ {i}) = 1 \mid \mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{CoT}} (c _ {i}) = 0) = \beta
$$

*   **Theorem 1 (GRPO Implicitly Incentivizes Correct Reasoning):**
    

$$
\mathbb {E} \left[ \hat {A} (y _ {i}) \mid \mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{CoT}} (c _ {i}) = 1 \right] > 0, \quad \mathbb {E} \left[ \hat {A} (y _ {i}) \mid \mathcal {I} _ {\mathrm{CoT}} (c _ {i}) = 0 \right] <   0
$$

*   **Expected Reward (Conditional):**
    

$$
\mathbb {E} [ R (y _ {i}) ] = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} \alpha & \text { if   } \mathcal {I} _ {C o T} (c _ {i}) = 1 \\ \beta & \text { if   } \mathcal {I} _ {C o T} (c _ {i}) = 0 \end{array} \right.
$$

*   **Group-level Expected Reward:**
    

$$
\mu = p _ {c} \alpha + (1 - p _ {c}) \beta
$$

*   **Variance of Reward:**
    

$$
\sigma^2 = (\alpha - \beta)^2 p_c (1 - p_c) + p_c \alpha(1 - \alpha) + (1 - p_c) \beta(1 - \beta)
$$

*   **Expected Advantage (Conditional, simplified for ideal scenario):**
    

$$
\mathbb{E}[\hat{A}(y_i) \mid \text{correct CoT}] \to \sqrt{\frac{1 - p_c}{p_c}}, \quad \mathbb{E}[\hat{A}(y_i) \mid \text{incorrect CoT}] \to -\sqrt{\frac{p_c}{1 - p_c}}
$$

**Key Quantitative Results and Numbers:**

*   **CoT-Pass@K on Math Tasks:** On AIME 2024 and AIME 2025, CoT-Pass@K results show a consistent and significant performance gap between the post-RLVR model (DAPO-Qwen-32B) and the base LLM (Qwen2.5-32B) across all K values (up to 1024). This gap is particularly pronounced on AIME 2025.
*   **Pass@K on Code Tasks:** AceReason-Nemotron-7B (post-RLVR) shows clear Pass@K improvements over DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B (pre-RLVR) on most LiveCodeBench versions, even for K up to 1024. Skywork-OR1-7B also shows a consistent Pass@K gap over DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B on LiveCodeBench-v6, especially for medium and hard problems.
*   **Training Dynamics:**
    *   For fully optimized training questions, $P(CA)^{(q)}$ (fraction of correct answers) almost reaches 1.0.
    *   $P(CC|CA)^{(q)}$ (fraction of correct CoTs within correct answers) shows improvement, with the median around 0.7 for fully optimized training questions.
    *   Generalization improvements in both Pass@K and CoT-Pass@K are observed from the early stages of training (within the first 20 training steps for AIME 2024).
*   **CoT Quality Evaluation:** SFT on DAPO CoT data matches the Pass@1 performance of DAPO-Qwen-32B, indicating that high-quality CoTs generated by RLVR can replicate reasoning capabilities through SFT.

**Stated Limitations:**

*   **LLM as CoT Verifier:** The primary limitation is the reliance on an LLM (DeepSeek-R1-0528-Qwen3-8B) as the verifier for CoT correctness, due to the prohibitive cost of manual checking. While a multi-verification approach is used to mitigate errors, the verifier is not infallible.
*   **Generalization Guarantee:** The theoretical framework explains the optimization process of RLVR but does not provide a formal guarantee for its generalization to unseen data; generalization is observed empirically.
*   **DAPO Limitations:** For fully optimized training questions where $P(CA)^{(q)}$ approaches 1.0, a non-negligible portion of imperfect CoTs (median $P(CC|CA)^{(q)}$ around 0.7) still exists. This suggests that answer-correctness-based rewards alone might not fully mitigate all unexpected reasoning behaviors.
*   **Benchmark Contamination:** Static benchmarks are susceptible to contamination, potentially undermining the reliability of observed improvements.
*   **Domain Mismatch:** On the Minerva benchmark, the post-RLVR model showed no improvement, likely due to a train-test domain mismatch (DAPO training data was restricted to math problems with integer answers, while Minerva includes physics problems and free-form answers).
*   **Distilled LLMs in Math:** For distilled LLMs in math domains, RLVR primarily delivers sampling efficiency improvements, and distinct CoT-Pass@K gaps for large K values are not observed, suggesting that distilled LLMs may already master major reasoning capabilities learnable with answer-correctness rewards.
