**Core Problem**
Deep reinforcement learning systems typically require well-specified reward functions, which are often complex, poorly defined, or impossible to hand-engineer for real-world tasks. Directly using human feedback as a reward signal is prohibitively expensive because modern RL agents require hundreds or thousands of hours of interaction. The core challenge is to scale human preference feedback to complex, high-dimensional environments while drastically reducing the volume of required human oversight, enabling non-expert users to train sophisticated agents without access to the true reward function.

**Method/Recipe Step by Step**
The algorithm operates through three asynchronous processes that iteratively refine a policy and a reward model:
1. **Trajectory Generation:** The current policy interacts with the environment to produce a batch of trajectory segments.
2. **Preference Elicitation:** Pairs of short trajectory segments (1–2 second video clips) are presented to a human overseer. The human indicates which segment is preferred, if they are equally good, or if they cannot compare them. These judgments are recorded as triples $(\tau_1, \tau_2, p)$, where $p$ is a probability distribution over the preferred segment.
3. **Policy Optimization:** The policy parameters are updated using a traditional RL algorithm (A2C for Atari, TRPO for robotics) to maximize the sum of predicted rewards from the reward model. Rewards are normalized to zero mean and constant standard deviation.
4. **Reward Model Fitting:** The reward function, parameterized by a deep neural network, is updated via supervised learning to minimize the discrepancy between its predictions and the collected human preferences.
5. **Query Selection:** To maximize information gain, the system samples many trajectory pairs and uses an ensemble of reward predictors to identify pairs with the highest variance in preference predictions. These high-uncertainty pairs are queried next.
6. **Ensemble & Regularization:** The reward predictor employs an ensemble of networks trained with bootstrapped data. Outputs are independently normalized and averaged. A validation set enforces regularization, and a 10% probability of uniform random human response is incorporated to account for labeling noise.

**Key Formulas**
The preference elicitation framework models human choices using the Bradley-Terry model, specialized to trajectory segments. The probability that a human prefers segment $\tau_1$ over $\tau_2$ is defined as:
$$P(\tau_1 \succ \tau_2) = \frac{e^{R(\tau_1)}}{e^{R(\tau_1)} + e^{R(\tau_2)}}$$
where $R(\tau)$ denotes the predicted reward for the segment. The reward network is trained by minimizing the cross-entropy loss between these softmax predictions and the actual human label distributions. This formulation treats reward differences as a preference ranking scale, analogous to Elo ratings.

**Key Quantitative Results and Numbers**
The approach requires feedback on less than 1% of the agent’s total environmental interactions. In simulated robotics (MuJoCo), 700 human queries nearly match the performance of RL trained with the true reward function. With 1,400 synthetic queries, the learned reward function slightly outperforms ground-truth RL, attributed to superior reward shaping. Human feedback efficiency ranged from half to equal ground-truth efficiency across tasks, significantly outperforming synthetic feedback on the Ant task due to explicit upright-stability preferences. In Atari environments, 5,500 human queries enabled substantial learning. Synthetic labels matched or approached RL performance on BeamRider and Pong with only 3,300 queries. On Seaquest and Qbert, synthetic feedback approached RL levels but learned more slowly, while SpaceInvaders and Breakout showed substantial improvement despite not matching RL. Novel complex behaviors (e.g., robot backflips, traffic-flow driving) were successfully learned with approximately one hour of human feedback.

**Stated Limitations**
The query selection strategy is explicitly described as a crude approximation of expected information value; ablation studies indicate it can impair performance in certain tasks. Training with learned reward functions exhibits higher variance and lower stability compared to direct RL optimization. The preference model in Equation 1 omits discounting, implicitly assuming human indifference to the temporal ordering of events within short clips. Additionally, the framework assumes human preferences are generated by a stationary reward function, though it notes that partially observable environments could require recurrent architectures. Finally, the method relies on the assumption that a constant 10% error rate adequately captures human labeling noise, which may not generalize to all domains.
