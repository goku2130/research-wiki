Deep reinforcement learning (RL) systems traditionally require well-specified reward functions, which are often impossible to hand-engineer for complex, real-world tasks. Direct human feedback as a reward signal is computationally prohibitive, requiring hundreds of hours of interaction. The core challenge is to scale human preference learning to modern deep RL architectures while drastically reducing feedback overhead, enabling non-expert users to teach sophisticated behaviors without explicit reward definitions.

The algorithm operates asynchronously through three coupled processes. First, a policy network $\pi$ interacts with the environment to generate trajectory segments, visualized as 1–2 second video clips. Second, a human overseer compares pairs of these clips, recording preferences as a distribution $\mu$ over the two options. Third, a reward predictor network $\hat{r}$ is trained via supervised learning to fit the accumulated preference database $\mathcal{D}$. The policy is then updated using standard RL algorithms (A2C for Atari, TRPO for robotics) to maximize the predicted rewards from $\hat{r}$. Reward predictions are normalized to zero mean and constant standard deviation. To select informative queries, the system samples candidate segment pairs and chooses those with the highest variance across an ensemble of reward predictors. The ensemble is trained with $\ell_2$ regularization, dropout, and a 10% uniform-response assumption to account for human error. The training pipeline employs a label annealing schedule, where the query rate decays inversely with elapsed timesteps to balance early predictor quality with later distributional adaptation. For continuous control tasks, TRPO utilizes a discount rate of 0.995 and an entropy bonus of 0.01 to maintain exploration under a non-stationary reward function, while Atari agents use A2C with a 0.99 discount rate and linearly decaying learning rates.

The preference predictor follows a Bradley-Terry model, estimating the probability that segment $\sigma^1$ is preferred over $\sigma^2$ as:
$$\hat{P}[\sigma^1 \succ \sigma^2] = \frac{\exp \sum \hat{r}(o_t^1, a_t^1)}{\exp \sum \hat{r}(o_t^1, a_t^1) + \exp \sum \hat{r}(o_t^2, a_t^2)}$$
The reward network is optimized by minimizing the cross-entropy loss against human labels:
$$\text{loss}(\hat{r}) = - \sum_{(\sigma^1, \sigma^2, \mu) \in \mathcal{D}} \mu(1) \log \hat{P}[\sigma^1 \succ \sigma^2] + \mu(2) \log \hat{P}[\sigma^2 \succ \sigma^1]$$

The approach reduces human oversight requirements to less than 1% of the agent's environmental interactions, cutting feedback costs by approximately three orders of magnitude. Training typically requires between 15 minutes and 5 hours of human time (300–3,000 queries). In MuJoCo simulated robotics, 700 human labels nearly match the performance of RL trained with the true reward function, while 1,400 labels occasionally outperform ground-truth RL due to improved reward shaping. On Atari games, 5,500 human labels match or exceed standard RL on titles like BeamRider, Pong, and Enduro, and yield substantial progress on others. The method successfully learns novel, complex behaviors from roughly one hour of feedback, including a Hopper robot performing continuous backflips (900 queries), a Half-Cheetah walking on one leg (800 queries), and an Enduro agent maintaining pace with traffic (~1,300 queries). Compute costs for training remain low, approximately $25 per day on standard cloud hardware.

The authors note several constraints. Offline training of the reward predictor fails catastrophically due to distributional non-stationarity, causing agents to exploit partial reward models (e.g., avoiding scoring in Pong rather than maximizing it), necessitating online, interleaved feedback. Query selection via ensemble disagreement is a crude approximation that can degrade performance in certain domains. Human labeling introduces noise, inconsistency, and "can't tell" responses, particularly in visually complex or context-dependent games like Qbert. Additionally, the pipeline currently approaches diminishing returns on sample complexity, as computational costs are becoming comparable to the cost of non-expert human feedback. The framework also assumes preferences can be reliably captured by short trajectory segments, which may not generalize to all long-horizon or highly sparse-reward tasks.
