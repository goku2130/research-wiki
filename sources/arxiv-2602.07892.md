The paper addresses the **alignment tax**, a well-documented phenomenon where safety post-training (e.g., SFT, DPO, RLHF) degrades the general utility of large language models. The authors reframe this trade-off as a **heterogeneous continual learning problem**, identifying gradient interference as a primary mechanism: because post-training sequentially shifts both data distributions and optimization objectives, safety-driven gradients often project onto parameter directions that encode pre-trained capabilities, causing capability regression. To mitigate this, they propose **Orthogonal Gradient Projection for Safety Alignment (OGPSA)**, a lightweight, plug-and-play update rule that constrains safety optimization to avoid first-order interference with general capabilities.

The OGPSA recipe proceeds as follows. First, the method maintains a low-rank general-capability subspace estimated from a small, diverse set of reference datasets $\mathcal{D}_{ref}^{(i)}$ targeting specific competencies (e.g., helpfulness, truthfulness). Every $K$ training steps, the algorithm computes reference gradients $\mathbf{g}^{(i)}(\theta)$ on mini-batches from these datasets and constructs an orthonormal basis $\mathbf{U}_\tau$ via Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization with a redundancy threshold $\delta$. Second, at each standard safety alignment step $t$, the algorithm computes the standard safety gradient $\mathbf{g}_{safe} = \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{safe}(\theta_t)$. Third, it projects this gradient onto the orthogonal complement of the estimated subspace, removing any component that would increase reference losses to first order. Finally, parameters are updated using the projected gradient. This procedure requires only periodic reference-gradient computations and low-rank inner products, avoiding large-scale data replay.

The mathematical foundation relies on first-order capability preservation. The alignment tax is defined as $\Delta_{tax} = \Phi(\theta_{pre}; \mathcal{D}_{eval}) - \Phi(\theta_{safe}; \mathcal{D}_{eval})$. Capability preservation is enforced by requiring $\langle \mathbf{g}^{(i)}(\theta), \Delta\theta \rangle = 0$ for all reference gradients, which is equivalent to constraining updates to $\mathcal{S}_{gen}(\theta)^\perp$, where $\mathcal{S}_{gen}(\theta) := \text{span}\{\mathbf{g}^{(1)}(\theta), \dots, \mathbf{g}^{(M)}(\theta)\}$. The projected update rule is $\Delta\theta = -\eta P_{\mathcal{S}_{gen}(\theta)^\perp}(\mathbf{g}_{safe}(\theta))$, computationally realized as $\tilde{\mathbf{g}}_{safe} = \mathbf{g}_{safe} - \mathbf{U}_\tau(\mathbf{U}_\tau^\top \mathbf{g}_{safe})$. The authors prove that this projection yields the steepest feasible descent direction under the orthogonality constraints.

Empirically, OGPSA consistently improves the safety–utility trade-off across Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Llama3.1-8B-Instruct under SFT, DPO, and sequential SFT→DPO pipelines. In the sequential SFT→DPO setting, the average performance gain over the instruct baseline increases from 33.98% to 42.74% on Qwen2.5-7B and from 19.74% to 32.98% on Llama3.1-8B. The method demonstrates high data efficiency, requiring only 100–200 reference samples per dimension to recover capability metrics effectively, compared to 10,000-sample replay baselines. Computationally, OGPSA reduces training time to 2 hours 56 minutes versus 4 hours 5 minutes for general-data mixing, while maintaining competitive safety scores and reducing optimization-based jailbreak success rates (e.g., I-GCG attack success drops to 26% under SFT+OGPSA versus 32% for standard SFT).

The authors explicitly acknowledge several limitations. OGPSA provides only a first-order local approximation and does not guarantee global safety or capability preservation. Its efficacy depends on the diversity of reference data; single-direction projections prove too narrow for broad capability retention. The method introduces minor computational overhead from periodic reference-gradient computation and projection operations. Furthermore, the approach has not been evaluated on larger architectures or comprehensive black-box adaptive safety benchmarks, and the authors caution that improved post-training utility does not equate to comprehensive robustness against all jailbreaks or deployment distribution shifts.
