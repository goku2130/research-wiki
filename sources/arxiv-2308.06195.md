This study addresses the characterization of hot stellar populations, specifically blue straggler stars (BSS), within the old open cluster Berkeley 39. BSS are rejuvenated main-sequence stars that appear brighter and bluer than the main-sequence turnoff, challenging standard single-star evolution models. Their formation is attributed to mass transfer in binaries, stellar mergers, or direct collisions. The core objective is to determine the fundamental physical parameters of BSS, detect unresolved hot companions via ultraviolet (UV) excess, and constrain formation pathways using multi-wavelength photometry.

The methodology follows a rigorous, multi-stage pipeline. First, optical astrometry from Gaia DR3 is processed using the ML-MOC algorithm, which integrates k-Nearest Neighbour classification and Gaussian mixture modeling to assign membership based on parallax and proper motion. This step identifies 861 cluster members, including 17 BSS candidates. Second, a comprehensive photometric dataset is assembled: UV fluxes from Swift/UVOT (UVW1, UVM2, UVW2) and GALEX; optical magnitudes from Gaia DR3 and Pan-STARRS; near-IR from 2MASS; and mid-IR from Spitzer/IRAC and WISE. Third, Spectral Energy Distributions (SEDs) are constructed using the Virtual Observatory SED Analyzer (VOSA). The pipeline requires input of source coordinates, compiled fluxes, a mean cluster distance of 3.8 kpc, and a visual extinction of $A_V = 0.51$ mag. To isolate potential UV excess, optical and infrared data points are initially fitted using Kurucz stellar atmosphere models. Metallicity is fixed at $[Fe/H] = 0$, aligning with the cluster’s measured value of $-0.20$, while effective temperature ($T_{\text{eff}}$) and surface gravity ($\log g$) are scanned across 3500–50000 K and 3–5, respectively. The optimal atmospheric model is selected via $\chi^2$ minimization, calculated as:
$$\chi_r^2 = \frac{1}{N - N_f} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{(F_{o,i} - M_d F_{m,i})^2}{\Sigma_{o,i}^2}$$
where $N$ denotes the total photometric data points, $N_f$ the fitted model parameters, $F_{o,i}$ and $F_{m,i}$ the observed and model fluxes, and $M_d = (\frac{r}{d})^2$ the geometric scaling factor dependent on stellar radius and distance. Finally, residuals are computed across all UV filters, and the Binary_SED_fit pipeline is deployed to model excess emission using Koester and Kurucz templates.

Quantitative results center on two BSS: BSS3 and BSS9. BSS9 exhibits no UV excess and is accurately reproduced by a single-component Kurucz fit. Conversely, BSS3 displays a pronounced UV excess, with a fractional residual exceeding 0.5 across all three Swift/UVOT filters. Its precise UV fluxes are measured at $9.83405 \times 10^{-16}$, $7.79188 \times 10^{-16}$, and $6.964300 \times 10^{-16}$ erg s$^{-1}$ cm$^{-2}$ Å$^{-1}$ in the UVW2, UVM2, and UVW1 bands, respectively. The host cluster is characterized as a $\sim 6$ Gyr-old, massive system ($\sim 2 \times 10^4 M_\odot$) with $[Fe/H] = -0.20$ located at $\sim 3.8$ kpc.

The analysis is constrained by several critical limitations. Single-component SED fitting fails to reproduce BSS3’s UV excess, and binary fitting attempts using standard stellar atmosphere models yield no physically viable solution. The absence of XMM and Chandra X-ray catalogs for this specific region prevents verification of a hot companion or the exclusion of chromospheric activity as an alternative driver of the excess emission. Consequently, the authors conclude that definitive characterization requires follow-up spectroscopic and photometric observations, specifically utilizing the 3.6 m Devasthal Optical Telescope, to constrain fundamental parameters and resolve the physical origin of the UV emission. The comprehensive analysis of the remaining BSS population is explicitly deferred to subsequent publications.
