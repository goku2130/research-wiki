The paper challenges the claim that large language models (LLMs) exhibit "emergent abilities"—sharp, unpredictable transitions in capability at specific scales. It argues these phenomena are likely artifacts of researcher-chosen evaluation metrics rather than fundamental shifts in model behavior. The authors formalize an alternative explanation using a mathematical model grounded in neural scaling laws. They assume per-token cross-entropy loss decreases smoothly as a power law with parameter count $N$. They then demonstrate how applying nonlinear or discontinuous metrics to these smooth per-token error rates distorts performance curves into sharp, unpredictable jumps. To validate this, the authors propose a three-step analytical recipe: first, they derive a mathematical mapping from scaling laws to task metrics; second, they re-evaluate fixed model outputs across alternative metrics to test whether apparent emergence evaporates; and third, they meta-analyze published benchmarks and intentionally induce artificial emergence in vision models to demonstrate metric-driven artifacts.

The mathematical model defines per-token cross-entropy as $\mathcal{L}_{CE}(N) = (N/c)^\alpha$, yielding a per-token correct probability of $p(\text{single token correct}) = \exp(-(N/c)^\alpha)$. Under a nonlinear metric like Accuracy for sequence length $L$, performance scales as $\text{Accuracy}(N) \approx \exp(-(N/c)^\alpha)^L$, producing geometric decay. Conversely, a linear metric like Token Edit Distance yields $\text{Token Edit Distance}(N) \approx L(1 - \exp(-(N/c)^\alpha))$, revealing smooth scaling. Discontinuous metrics like Multiple Choice Grade and Exact String Match are defined as binary indicators: $\text{Multiple Choice Grade} = 1$ if the highest probability mass is on the correct option, else $0$; $\text{Exact String Match} = 1$ if the output exactly matches the target, else $0$. The emergence score is calculated as $\text{Emergence Score} = \frac{\text{sign}(\arg \max_i y_i - \arg \min_i y_i)(\max_i y_i - \min_i y_i)}{\sqrt{\text{Median}(\{(y_i - y_{i-1})^2\}_i)}}$.

Re-analysis of GPT-3/InstructGPT arithmetic tasks shows that switching from Accuracy to Token Edit Distance eliminates sharp transitions, replacing them with predictable, continuous improvements. Increasing test dataset resolution reveals that smaller models maintain above-chance accuracy under nonlinear metrics, contradicting claims of zero capability. In the BIG-Bench meta-analysis, >92% of claimed emergent abilities occur exclusively under Multiple Choice Grade and Exact String Match. Among 39 preferred metrics, at most five exhibit emergence, with hand-annotated data confirming only four. When LaMDA’s performance is re-evaluated on tasks showing emergence under Multiple Choice Grade, switching to the continuous Brier Score completely removes the emergent transition. Similarly, applying custom discontinuous metrics to CIFAR100 autoencoders and Omniglot Transformers induces sharp, unpredictable capability jumps that qualitatively match published LLM emergence claims. The custom discontinuous metric for vision tasks, Reconstruction$_c$, is defined as $\frac{1}{N} \sum_n \mathbb{I}[||x_n - \hat{x}_n||^2 < c]$, which thresholds squared reconstruction error to induce sharp transitions. The authors further note that ROUGE-L-Sum disproportionately benefits models with lower error rates by stitching together longest common subsequences across references, amplifying apparent emergence.

The authors acknowledge several constraints. The mathematical model assumes per-token errors are independent and constant, which is empirically false but serves as a qualitative approximation. Resolution for measuring per-token error is fundamentally limited by $1/(NL)$, meaning small test sets artificially depress smaller models’ apparent performance. The framework does not account for autoregressive over- or under-generation (additions/deletions). Furthermore, the authors caution that massive multiple comparisons across ~$10^6$ task-metric-model triplets in benchmarks like BIG-Bench may inflate false-positive emergence claims. They emphasize that benchmark construction must treat tasks and metrics as distinct choices, and that proper controls are critical when evaluating model capabilities. They explicitly state their work does not prove LLMs cannot exhibit true emergence, only that previously reported instances are likely metric-induced mirages.
