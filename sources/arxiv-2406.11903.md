**Core Problem:** The financial sector is defined by high complexity, uncertainty, and rapid data evolution, necessitating advanced computational frameworks capable of processing vast unstructured corpora, extracting nuanced market sentiment, and supporting real-time analytical decision-making. Existing literature frequently lacks a unified examination of practical deployment constraints, specialized benchmarks, and domain-specific adaptation strategies. This survey systematically evaluates the integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into financial workflows to bridge academic research and industry implementation.

**Method/Analytical Framework:** The authors establish a structured taxonomy categorizing foundation architectures, adaptation protocols, and application domains. Base models include transformer-based families (GPT-series, BERT, T5, ELECTRA, BLOOM, Llama-series) and their financial variants (FinBERT, BloombergGPT, FinGPT, InvestLM). Adaptation strategies are contrasted between zero-shot/few-shot inference and fine-tuning, emphasizing instruction tuning, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and parameter-efficient methods like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). The survey dissects applications across five primary domains: linguistic tasks (document summarization, information extraction, NER, knowledge graph construction), sentiment analysis (social media, news, corporate disclosures, policy indicators), financial time series analysis (forecasting, anomaly detection, classification, augmentation, imputation), financial reasoning (planning, recommendation, decision support), and agent-based modeling (trading, market simulation, automated processes). Methodological implementations include structural chunking for RAG, layout-aware attention mechanisms for multimodal documents, hybrid-tuning strategies to prevent catastrophic forgetting, and multi-agent frameworks that fuse statistical methods with AI analytics.

**Key Formulas:** The source text does not contain explicit mathematical formulations or equations. Technical descriptions are provided qualitatively through architectural specifications, training objectives (e.g., span corruption for T5, generator-discriminator training for ELECTRA), and algorithmic workflows rather than closed-form mathematical derivations.

**Key Quantitative Results:** The survey documents several empirical benchmarks and model specifications. The Knowledge Graph Enriched BERT (KGEB) model achieves 91.98% accuracy and a 90.89% F1 score in industry classification tasks. An ESG classifier trained on Korean corporate sustainability reports attains 86.66% accuracy for four-class categorization. Model parameter scales range from 7B to 176B, with Llama 2 incorporating a 40% expanded pretraining corpus and doubled context length. In sentiment-driven forecasting, GPT-4 significantly outperforms naive buy-and-hold strategies for Apple and Tesla stocks using microblogging data, and surpasses traditional baselines in predicting stock returns from news headlines, particularly following negative news and for smaller-cap equities. Conversely, zero-shot multimodal stock movement prediction using ChatGPT underperforms compared to traditional machine learning models. Smaller fine-tuned models trained via in-context learning and chain-of-thought reasoning achieve competitive performance with minimal human annotation. Frameworks like PloutosGen integrate multimodal expert pools for interpretable stock prediction, while multi-agent LLM systems enhance S&P 500 anomaly detection efficiency.

**Stated Limitations:** The authors identify critical technical and operational constraints. Backtesting methodologies are vulnerable to lookahead bias, while production deployment faces inference latency, computational costs, and data pollution risks. LLMs struggle with complex PDF layouts containing charts and tables, often losing spatial information during text conversion. Personal financial advisory chatbots exhibit mathematical calculation errors, lack visual explanatory aids, and perform poorly on non-English dialects. Financial reasoning models require human oversight to mitigate hallucination and ensure regulatory compliance. Time series forecasting via LLMs remains debated, with direct application showing inconsistent efficacy compared to specialized temporal models. Additional challenges include signal decay, uncertainty quantification, interpretability, ethical concerns, privacy preservation, and susceptibility to adversarial manipulation, though LLMs demonstrate improved resilience over keyword-based lexicons. Multimodal integration and real-time reasoning capabilities also face scalability and cost barriers that limit widespread institutional adoption.
