The source addresses the core problem of faithfully reproducing OpenAI’s 2019 RLHF with PPO methodology using modern deep learning frameworks. Despite the algorithm's conceptual clarity, the original TensorFlow 1.x codebase contains numerous unreported implementation details that drastically alter training dynamics when ported to PyTorch or HuggingFace Transformers. The authors systematically reverse-engineer these details to achieve nearly identical learning curves on stylistic tasks (sentiment and descriptiveness), demonstrating that framework-agnostic reproduction fails without precise alignment of tokenization, sampling, normalization, and optimizer behavior.

The reproduction recipe proceeds through several critical stages. First, tokenization concatenates query and response into a single `query_response` sequence, padded with a dedicated `[PAD]` token rather than `EOS`. Sequences are truncated or right-padded to a fixed `query_length`, and position IDs are computed via exclusive cumulative sums on the attention mask for forward passes, but explicitly disabled during `generate()` to prevent attention collapse. Generation is forced to fixed-length responses by disabling `eos_token_id` and `pad_token_id` in the generation config, using categorical sampling with `top_k=0` and `top_p=1.0`. Second, the reward model extracts the scalar reward from the final response token. Rewards are normalized using a fixed reference policy $\rho$, applying affine scaling to target a mean of zero and standard deviation of one. Third, the PPO policy training loop employs micro-batching with gradient accumulation and per-epoch batch shuffling. Per-token KL penalties are computed as $\text{kl} = \text{new\_logprobs} - \text{logprobs}$, scaled by coefficient $\beta$, and added to the reward model score. Advantages and rewards undergo batch whitening, with rewards shifted to zero mean and advantages retaining their original mean. An adaptive KL controller dynamically adjusts $\beta$ based on the ratio of current to target KL divergence. Finally, optimizer alignment is critical: PyTorch’s Adam implementation scales $\varepsilon$ by $\sqrt{1-\beta_2^t}$, causing more aggressive early updates than TensorFlow’s constant $\varepsilon$. Reproduction requires a custom TF-style Adam implementation to match original dynamics.

Key formulas governing the pipeline include reward normalization $\text{reward} = \text{reward} \times \text{reward\_gain} + \text{reward\_bias}$, where $\text{reward\_gain} = \sigma_{\mathcal{T}} / \sigma_{\rho}$ and $\text{reward\_bias} = \mu_{\mathcal{T}} - \text{reward\_gain} \times \mu_{\rho}$ (with target $\mu_{\mathcal{T}}=0$, $\sigma_{\mathcal{T}}=1$). The whitening operation is defined as $\text{whitened} = (\text{values} - \text{mean}) \times \text{rsqrt}(\text{var} + 10^{-8})$, conditionally shifted. The adaptive KL update follows $\text{mult} = 1 + \text{proportional\_error} \times \frac{n_{\text{steps}}}{\text{horizon}}$, where $\text{proportional\_error} = \text{clip}(\frac{\text{current}}{\text{target}} - 1, -0.2, 0.2)$. Training uses $\text{init\_kl\_coef}=0.15$, $\text{target}=6$, and $\text{horizon}=10000$, with fixed sequence lengths of 88 tokens (64 query + 24 response).

Quantitatively, the reproduced codebase matches OpenAI’s learning curves nearly identically. However, direct comparison of optimizer behavior after two epochs reveals stark differences: PyTorch Adam yields a `policy/approxkl` of $\approx 0.00237$ versus TF1’s $\approx 0.00037$, a `ratio_max` of $\approx 1.812$ versus $\approx 1.227$, and a `logprob_diff_var` $\approx 6\times$ higher, indicating significantly more aggressive policy updates and frequent clipping. These discrepancies underscore why framework-agnostic reproduction fails without precise optimizer and normalization alignment.

The authors note several limitations. The original OpenAI datasets are partially corrupted, necessitating replacement with HuggingFace equivalents, which may introduce minor performance variations. The work is explicitly educational and not optimized for large-scale or parameter-efficient fine-tuning, for which dedicated libraries like `huggingface/trl` are recommended. Full reproduction also depends on specific legacy infrastructure (e.g., TF1.x, `p3dn.24xlarge` instances) and careful hyperparameter tuning to avoid KL divergence collapse.
