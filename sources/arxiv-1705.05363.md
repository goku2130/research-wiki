Deep reinforcement learning agents typically fail in environments with sparse or absent extrinsic rewards. Directly predicting future pixel states is computationally intractable and highly sensitive to stochastic, uncontrollable environmental variations (e.g., moving leaves, distractors), which can trap agents in artificial curiosity loops or stall exploration. To address this, the authors propose the Intrinsic Curiosity Module (ICM), which generates curiosity-driven intrinsic rewards by measuring prediction error in a self-supervised feature space.

The ICM recipe operates through four sequential steps. First, an inverse dynamics model is trained to predict the executed action $a_t$ given consecutive raw states $s_t$ and $s_{t+1}$. Because the network only needs to predict the action, it is forced to learn a compressed feature embedding $\phi(s)$ that captures exclusively environmental factors relevant to the agent's motor control, ignoring nuisance variations. Second, a forward dynamics model is trained to predict the feature representation of the next state, $\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1})$, using the current feature $\phi(s_t)$ and the executed action $a_t$. Third, the squared prediction error of this forward model in the learned feature space is computed and scaled to produce an intrinsic curiosity reward $r_t^i$. Fourth, the agent's policy is trained via asynchronous advantage actor-critic (A3C) to maximize the sum of this intrinsic reward and any available extrinsic reward. The architecture employs four convolutional layers with ELU activations for state encoding, followed by fully connected layers for the inverse and forward modules.

The mathematical formulation defines the inverse dynamics optimization as $\min_{\theta_I} L_I(\hat{a}_t, a_t)$, where $\hat{a}_t = g(s_t, s_{t+1}; \theta_I)$. The forward dynamics model minimizes the mean squared error $L_F(\phi(s_t), \hat{\phi}(s_{t+1})) = \frac{1}{2} \|\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1}) - \phi(s_{t+1})\|_2^2$, where $\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1}) = f(\phi(s_t), a_t; \theta_F)$. The intrinsic reward is explicitly defined as $r_t^i = \frac{\eta}{2} \|\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1}) - \phi(s_{t+1})\|_2^2$. The joint optimization problem balances policy gradient and module losses: $\min_{\theta_F, \theta_I, \theta_P} \left[ -\lambda \mathbb{E}_{\pi(s_t; \theta_P)}[\Sigma_t r_t] + (1 - \beta)L_I + \beta L_F \right]$.

Quantitative evaluations on VizDoom and Super Mario Bros demonstrate ICM's efficacy. In VizDoom's sparse reward setting, ICM+A3C achieved a 100.0% mean success rate, surpassing vanilla A3C (0.0%), TRPO (26.0%), and VIME+TRPO (46.1%). ICM also demonstrated robustness to 40% visual noise distractors, whereas pixel-based curiosity baselines failed completely. In a no-reward setting, the agent learned to traverse over 30% of Level-1 in Super Mario Bros without external supervision. For generalization, curiosity-pretrained policies fine-tuned on novel VizDoom maps and Mario levels consistently outperformed agents trained from scratch, confirming the acquisition of transferable exploration skills.

The authors identify two primary limitations. First, in Super Mario Bros, the agent cannot progress past a specific pit requiring a precise 15–20 key press sequence; upon falling, the agent receives no gradient signal, halting further exploration. Second, the method does not directly extend to environments where opportunities for interaction are inherently rare. Additionally, fine-tuning on highly difficult levels (e.g., Mario Level-3) can cause a "curiosity blockade," where the agent stops making progress, receives near-zero intrinsic rewards, and experiences policy degeneration analogous to boredom.
