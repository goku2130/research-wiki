# GM-PRM: A Generative Multimodal Process Reward Model for Multimodal Mathematical Reasoning

### Core Problem
Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) often fail at complex, multi-step mathematical reasoning due to cascading errors in visual perception or logical deduction. While existing Process Reward Models (PRMs) provide step-by-step supervision, they typically function as binary verifiers that assign a scalar score or a correct/incorrect label. These models lack interpretability and the ability to correct identified errors, rendering them passive judges in test-time strategies like Best-of-N (BoN) sampling.

### Method
The authors propose the **Generative Multimodal Process Reward Model (GM-PRM)**, which transforms the PRM into an active collaborator capable of providing detailed critiques and generating corrected reasoning steps.

#### 1. Data Construction
The training dataset was curated from the VisualPRM400K dataset, focusing on plane geometry and function-related problems. The pipeline consists of:
*   **Generative Analysis:** GPT-4o is used to analyze each reasoning step $r_i$ across four dimensions: **Step Intent** ($SI$), **Image Alignment** ($IA$), **Reasoning Logic** ($RL$), and **Step Refinement** ($RS$).
*   **Filtering via MC Estimation:** To ensure reliability, the authors employ Monte Carlo (MC) "hard estimation." A step $r_i$ is deemed correct if at least one subsequent solution generated by a completer model reaches the correct final answer $a^*$:

$$
l_{i}^{H E}=\begin{cases} 1, & \exists a_{j}, a_{j}=a^{*} \\ 0, & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
$$

*   **Consistency Check:** Only samples where the GPT-4o judgment and the MC estimation label are consistent are included in the final training set.

#### 2. Model Training
GM-PRM is built upon the **Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct** base model. The model is trained via supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on 19,614 samples (9,061 incorrect and 10,553 correct solutions). All parameters are trainable except for the frozen Vision Transformer (ViT) encoder.

#### 3. Refined Best-of-N (Refined-BoN) Inference
Instead of passive selection, Refined-BoN iteratively improves the solution pool:
1.  **Initial Generation:** The policy model generates $N/2$ initial solutions.
2.  **Active Refinement:** GM-PRM evaluates these solutions. If an error is found at step $r_i$, GM-PRM generates a corrected version of that step.
3.  **Guided Regeneration:** The policy model generates the remaining $N/2$ solutions using the corrected step and all preceding valid steps as a prompt.
4.  **Selection:** The optimal solution is selected based on the average probability of the "Correct" token across all steps.

### Key Formulas
The general inference of the GM-PRM is defined as:

$$
f_{\text{G M-P R M}}:(Q, I, R) \mapsto(c_{1}, j_{1}, \ldots, c_{T}, j_{T})
$$

where $Q$ is the question, $I$ is the image, $R$ is the reasoning chain, $c_i$ is the critique, and $j_i$ is the judgment for step $i$.

The GPT-4o data generation function is represented as:

$$
\mathcal{F}: (Q, I, R \parallel P) \mapsto \left\{S I_{i}, I A_{i}, R L_{i}, F J_{i} \right\}_{i=1}^{t}
$$

where $FJ_i$ is the final judgment and $t$ is the index of the first incorrect step or the final step.

### Quantitative Results
GM-PRM demonstrated state-of-the-art performance across five benchmarks (MathVista, MathVision, MathVerse, DynaMath, and WeMath). 

**Average Accuracy Improvements:**
*   **MiniCPM-V2.6-8B:** +5.9% (notably +12.4% on WeMath)
*   **Llama-3.2-11B-Vision:** +4.5%
*   **Qwen2.5-VL-7B:** +4.5%
*   **InternVL3-8B:** +5.6%

**Additional Findings:**
*   **Data Efficiency:** High performance was achieved using a relatively small training set of $\approx 20\text{K}$ samples.
*   **Model Scaling:** Models with lower baseline performance benefited more from the refinement process (e.g., MiniCPM-V2.6-8B saw a 21.0% relative gain).
*   **Diversity:** Refined-BoN increased average Pass@8 scores for MiniCPM-V2.6-8B (+0.9), Llama-3.2-11B-Vision (+1.3), and InternVL3-8B (+0.9) compared to standard BoN.

### Limitations
The authors note that improvements on the **MathVision** benchmark were more modest (+1.9 to +3.9 points) compared to other datasets, suggesting that the visual reasoning capabilities of the baseline policy models may already be relatively strong in that specific domain.
