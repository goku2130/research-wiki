# CoPRIS: Efficient and Stable Reinforcement Learning via Concurrency-Controlled Partial Rollout with Importance Sampling

### Core Problem
Reinforcement learning (RL) post-training for Large Language Models (LLMs) typically relies on synchronous systems where training waits for an entire batch of rollouts to complete. This creates a "long-tail problem": because response lengths vary significantly across prompts, GPUs that finish early remain idle while waiting for a few exceptionally long trajectories to conclude, leading to severe computational inefficiency. While asynchronous "partial rollout" strategies exist, they often suffer from uncontrolled concurrency—leading to memory overflow and expensive key-value (KV) recomputation—and training instability caused by the distribution mismatch of off-policy trajectories.

### Method
CoPRIS addresses these inefficiencies through a framework that integrates concurrency control with importance sampling. The process follows these steps:

1.  **Concurrency-Controlled Generation**: The system maintains a fixed number of concurrent rollout requests $N'$ within the inference engines. When a trajectory finishes, a new request is immediately dispatched, ensuring constant hardware utilization and preventing memory overflow.
2.  **Early Termination**: The rollout process halts immediately once the required batch size $B$ for a training step is collected, rather than waiting for all active trajectories to finish.
3.  **Buffering of Partial Trajectories**: Trajectories that are unfinished at the time of early termination are stored in a buffer $\mathcal{B}$ along with their log probabilities computed under the policy model active at the time of generation.
4.  **Prioritized Resumption**: In subsequent rollout stages, the inference engine prioritizes resuming these buffered partial trajectories before starting new ones.
5.  **Cross-stage Importance Sampling Correction**: To mitigate the bias introduced by trajectories generated by previous policies, CoPRIS applies importance sampling. It concatenates the buffered log probabilities from various stages and compares them against the current policy.

### Key Formulas
The log-probability of a trajectory $\tau_i$ is represented as the concatenation of log probabilities $\mathbf{L}_i^{(k)}$ generated by policy $\pi_{\theta^{(k)}}$ at each stage $k$:

$$
\mathbf{L}_{i}=\text{c o n c a t}\big(\mathbf{L}_{i}^{(1)},\,\mathbf{L}_{i}^{(2)},\,\ldots,\,\mathbf{L}_{i}^{(K)}\big)
$$

To correct the distribution mismatch during training, the importance ratio $r_{i,t}(\theta)$ for each token is calculated as:

$$
r_{i,t}(\theta)=\frac{\pi_{\theta}(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{o l d}}}(o_{i,t}\mid q,o_{i,<t})}=\text{e x p}\bigl(\mathbf{L}_{i,t}^{(\theta)}-\mathbf{L}_{i,t}\bigr)
$$

These ratios are used to reweight the objectives during policy optimization.

### Quantitative Results
CoPRIS was evaluated on mathematical reasoning benchmarks (AIME, AMC, MinervaMath, OlympiadBench) using Distill-Qwen (1.5B, 7B) and Qwen3-8B models.

*   **Training Speedup**: CoPRIS achieved an end-to-end speedup of **1.58$\times$ to 1.94$\times$** compared to the synchronous veRL baseline while maintaining comparable or superior performance.
*   **Context Length Scalability**: Speedups increased near-linearly with context length, rising from **1.27$\times$ at 8K tokens to 2.26$\times$ at 40K tokens**.
*   **Model Size Scalability**: Across model sizes of 1.5B, 7B, and 14B, the system maintained throughput speedups between **1.57$\times$ and 1.85$\times$**.
*   **Performance**: For the Distill-Qwen-7B model, CoPRIS improved the average pass@1 accuracy from 52.66% (veRL) to **53.68%**.

### Limitations
The authors identify two primary limitations:
1.  **Entropy Sensitivity**: The effectiveness of CoPRIS depends on the initial model entropy. While high-entropy models benefit from off-policy exploration, lower-entropy models may require more conservative tuning of the off-policy ratio to prevent instability.
2.  **Static Concurrency**: The use of a fixed concurrency level $N'$ may be suboptimal for larger models, as it can trigger memory-induced recomputation overhead. The authors suggest that dynamically adjusting the concurrency limit based on model size and available resources would further optimize performance.
