**Core Problem**
The source addresses the challenge of enhancing multi-step reasoning in large language models (LLMs) through process reward models (PRMs). While PRMs offer step-level feedback that can improve credit assignment compared to outcome reward models (ORMs), two primary bottlenecks exist: the non-scalability of collecting dense, per-step human annotations, and the historically limited performance gains when training PRMs on automatically labeled data. The central research question is how to effectively design process rewards to maximize utility during both test-time search and reinforcement learning (RL).

**Method and Recipe**
The authors propose a structured pipeline centered on measuring "progress" rather than static correctness. The methodology proceeds as follows:
1. **Progress Redefinition:** Process rewards are redefined to quantify the change in the probability of generating a correct final response before and after executing a reasoning step.
2. **Prover Policy Isolation:** Progress evaluation is strictly decoupled from the base policy by requiring assessment under a distinct prover policy.
3. **Theoretical Prover Characterization:** The authors derive theoretical conditions defining the set of "good" prover policies. These conditions are proven to enhance exploration during test-time search and online RL.
4. **PAV Training:** Process Advantage Verifiers (PAVs) are trained to predict the computed progress signals generated by the prover policies.
5. **Policy Optimization:** The trained PAVs are deployed either as scoring functions for test-time search or as dense reward signals for online RL fine-tuning of the base policy.

**Key Formulas and Mathematical Concepts**
While the abstract does not provide explicit mathematical notation, it formally characterizes progress as the difference in the likelihood of producing a correct response before and after a step, aligning with step-level advantages in RL:

$$
A_{\text{step}} = P(\text{correct} \mid \text{after step}) - P(\text{correct} \mid \text{before step})
$$

This formulation ensures that the verifier rewards transitions that demonstrably increase the trajectory's probability of reaching a correct outcome, directly mapping to the step-level advantages required for effective policy optimization.

**Key Quantitative Results**
Empirical validation demonstrates substantial improvements over ORMs when using PAVs:
- **Test-Time Search:** Search guided by PAVs achieves $>8\%$ higher accuracy and operates $1.5$ to $5\times$ more compute-efficiently than ORM-guided search.
- **Online RL:** Fine-tuning with dense PAV rewards yields a $5$ to $6\times$ improvement in sample efficiency and a $>6\%$ accuracy gain relative to ORMs.
- **Prover Dynamics:** Theoretical and empirical results indicate that weak prover policies can substantially improve a stronger base policy, challenging the assumption that prover quality must strictly exceed base policy capability.

**Stated Limitations**
The provided text does not explicitly enumerate limitations of the proposed PAV framework. It does, however, contextualize the work within two acknowledged constraints of prior approaches: the inherent unscaleability of dense human per-step labeling, and the historically limited performance gains achieved by training PRMs on automatically generated labels. These factors motivate the shift toward progress-based advantage verification but are not framed as direct limitations of the authors' methodology.
