The paper "Fine-Tuning Language Models from Human Preferences" by Ziegler et al. (2019) addresses the problem of applying reinforcement learning (RL) to natural language tasks where reward functions are inherently subjective and best defined by human judgment, rather than algorithmic metrics or readily available supervised datasets. The core problem is to effectively train large generative language models using human preferences to achieve desired stylistic or summarization outcomes.

The method involves a two-stage process:
1.  **Reward Model Training**:
    *   **Data Collection**: Gather samples $(x, y_0, y_1, y_2, y_3)$ where $x$ is an input (e.g., text excerpt, article) and $y_i$ are four possible continuations/summaries generated by the current language model policy $\rho$.
    *   **Human Labeling**: Human labelers are asked to choose the "best" continuation/summary $y_b$ from the four options for each input $x$.
    *   **Reward Model Fitting**: A reward model $r: X \times Y \to \mathbb{R}$ is trained on the collected dataset $S$ of $(x, y_0, y_1, y_2, y_3, b)$ tuples using the following loss function:

$$
\text{loss}(r)=\mathbb{E}_{\left(x,\left\{y_{i}\right\}_{i}, b\right) \sim S}\left[\text{log}\frac{e^{r(x,y_{b})}}{\sum_{i}e^{r(x,y_{i})}}\right]
$$

        The reward model is initialized as a random linear function of the final embedding output of the language model and normalized to have mean 0 and variance 1.

2.  **Policy Fine-tuning (Reinforcement Learning)**:
    *   The pretrained language model (policy $\pi$) is fine-tuned using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO2) to optimize the learned reward model $r$.
    *   A Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence penalty is added to the reward function to prevent the fine-tuned policy $\pi$ from drifting too far from the original pretrained policy $\rho$. The modified reward function is:

$$
R(x,y)=r(x,y)-\beta\log\frac{\pi(y|x)}{\rho(y|x)}.
$$

        The coefficient $\beta$ can be constant or dynamically varied to target a specific KL divergence value using a log-space proportional controller:

$$
e_{t}=\text{clip}\left(\frac{\text{K L}(\pi_{t},\rho)-\text{K L}_{\text{t a r g e t}}}{\text{K L}_{\text{t a r g e t}}},-0.2,0.2\right)
$$

        where $K_{\beta}=0.1$. This KL term also encourages coherence and topicality in stylistic tasks.
    *   **Online vs. Offline Data Collection**: For summarization, online data collection is used, where human data is continuously gathered by sampling from the improving policy $\pi$ and retraining the reward model. For stylistic continuation, offline data collection (training the reward model once on data from the initial language model) is found to be sufficient.

**Key Quantitative Results and Numbers:**

*   **Stylistic Continuation (Sentiment & Descriptiveness)**:
    *   Achieved good results with only 5,000 human comparisons (each choosing the best of 4 continuations).
    *   For positive sentiment, the fine-tuned model was preferred by humans 86% of the time vs. zero-shot and 77% vs. supervised sentiment network fine-tuning.
    *   Performance with 5k, 10k, and 20k reward model training samples was similar, degrading only for less than 5k samples.
    *   Offline data collection worked similarly well for stylistic tasks.
*   **Summarization (TL;DR & CNN/Daily Mail)**:
    *   Models trained with 60,000 human comparisons.
    *   Human labelers preferred the policy trained with online data collection 71% of the time for the TL;DR dataset compared to offline models.
    *   **ROUGE Scores (Table 4)**:
        *   For TL;DR, supervised+60k fine-tune achieved R-1: 18.434, R-2: 3.542, R-L: 15.457, R-AVG: 12.478.
        *   For CNN/Daily Mail, supervised+60k fine-tune achieved R-1: 40.093, R-2: 17.611, R-L: 37.104, R-AVG: 31.603.
        *   Lead-3 baseline often outperformed RL-only models and sometimes even supervised+RL models in ROUGE.
    *   **Human Evaluations (Table 5)**:
        *   60k online model reliably beat zero-shot and supervised baselines.
        *   60k online model beat human-written reference summaries 96% of the time for TL;DR and 84% of the time for CNN/Daily Mail.
    *   **Copying Behavior**: 60k RL fine-tuned models were almost entirely extractive, copying whole sentences 71% of the time for TL;DR and 98% for CNN/Daily Mail.
    *   **Accuracy (Table 8)**: 60k fine-tuned models were 90% accurate on TL;DR and 95% accurate on CNN/Daily Mail (judged by authors on 30 articles). Supervised and supervised+60k fine-tuned models were less accurate (e.g., 11/30 for supervised+60k fine-tuned on TL;DR).

**Stated Limitations:**

*   **Data Quality and Ambiguity**: Human judgment tasks are subjective and multidimensional, leading to significant disagreement even among consistent labelers (e.g., authors agree ~60% of the time, authors vs. Scale labelers ~38-46%). This complicates quality control and makes consistent labeling difficult.
*   **Online Data Collection Challenges**:
    *   **Software Complexity**: Interleaving data gathering, reward model training, and RL fine-tuning creates a significantly more complex distributed system, slowing development and making debugging harder.
    *   **Quality Control Issues**: Difficult to maintain high data quality over long periods in a low-latency, online setting. Regressions were often detected late.
*   **Overfitting of Reward Model**: The massive imbalance between reward model samples (at most 60k) and policy episodes (2M) makes it challenging to maintain performance without overfitting the reward model.
*   **Parameter Sharing Overfitting**: Sharing parameters between the reward model and policy (e.g., using the same base language model) can lead to overfitting of the reward model to the policy's outputs.
*   **Vulnerability to Bugs**: A bug that flipped the sign of both reward and KL penalty led to the model optimizing for maximally bad (e.g., sexually explicit) output, demonstrating the need for robust safety mechanisms.
*   **Summarization Model Behavior**: The RL fine-tuned summarization models often "degenerated to copying" whole sentences, becoming "smart copiers" rather than abstractive summarizers. While this led to high accuracy, it was not the desired abstractive behavior.
*   **ROUGE vs. Human Preference Discrepancy**: ROUGE scores did not always align with human preferences, highlighting the limitations of algorithmic metrics for subjective tasks.
