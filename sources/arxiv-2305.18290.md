The paper "Direct Preference Optimization: Your Language Model is Secretly a Reward Model" introduces Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), a novel algorithm for fine-tuning large language models (LMs) using human preferences without the complexities of traditional Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

**Core Problem:**
Traditional RLHF for aligning LMs with human preferences is a multi-stage, complex, and often unstable process. It typically involves:
1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) of a pre-trained LM ($\pi^{\mathrm{SFT}}$).
2. Training a separate reward model ($r_\phi(x,y)$) on human preference data, which involves sampling pairs of responses and labeling them as preferred ($y_w$) or dispreferred ($y_l$). This reward model is often initialized from the SFT model.
3. Fine-tuning the LM using Reinforcement Learning (RL), typically Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), to maximize the learned reward while maintaining proximity to the original SFT model via a KL-divergence constraint. This RL phase is computationally expensive and can be unstable due to the discrete nature of language generation and the need for sampling.

**Method/Recipe Step by Step:**
DPO simplifies the preference learning pipeline by reparameterizing the reward model in a way that allows the optimal policy to be extracted in closed form. This eliminates the need for explicit reward model training and the subsequent RL fine-tuning phase.

1.  **SFT (Optional but common):** Start with a supervised fine-tuned language model $\pi^{\mathrm{SFT}}$. This model serves as the reference policy $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$. If $\pi^{\mathrm{SFT}}$ is not available, $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ can be initialized by maximizing the likelihood of preferred completions $(x, y_w)$ from the preference dataset.
2.  **Preference Data Collection:** Collect a dataset of human preferences $\mathcal{D} = \{x^{(i)}, y_w^{(i)}, y_l^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N$, where $x$ is a prompt, $y_w$ is the preferred response, and $y_l$ is the dispreferred response. These pairs are typically generated by prompting the $\pi^{\mathrm{SFT}}$ model and having humans label preferences.
3.  **DPO Optimization:** Optimize the language model $\pi_\theta$ directly using a simple binary cross-entropy loss function derived from the Bradley-Terry preference model. This loss implicitly optimizes for the same objective as existing RLHF algorithms (reward maximization with a KL-divergence constraint).

**Key Formulas in LaTeX:**

*   **Bradley-Terry Preference Model (Traditional RLHF):**
    The human preference distribution $p^*$ is modeled as:

$$
p ^ {*} (y _ {1} \succ y _ {2} \mid x) = \frac {\exp \left(r ^ {*} (x , y _ {1})\right)}{\exp \left(r ^ {*} (x , y _ {1})\right) + \exp \left(r ^ {*} (x , y _ {2})\right)}
$$

*   **Reward Model Loss (Traditional RLHF):**
    The negative log-likelihood loss for training the reward model $r_\phi(x,y)$ is:

$$
\mathcal {L} _ {R} (r _ {\phi}, \mathcal {D}) = - \mathbb {E} _ {(x, y _ {w}, y _ {l}) \sim \mathcal {D}} [ \log \sigma (r _ {\phi} (x, y _ {w}) - r _ {\phi} (x, y _ {l})) ]
$$

    where $\sigma$ is the logistic function.
*   **RL Objective (Traditional RLHF):**
    The objective for fine-tuning the policy $\pi_\theta$ with RL is:

$$
\max _ {\pi_ {\theta}} \mathbb {E} _ {x \sim \mathcal {D}, y \sim \pi_ {\theta} (y | x)} [ r _ {\phi} (x, y) ] - \beta \mathbb {D} _ {\mathrm{KL}} [ \pi_ {\theta} (y \mid x) | | \pi_ {\text {ref}} (y \mid x) ]
$$

    where $\beta$ controls the KL-divergence constraint.
*   **Optimal Policy in Closed Form (DPO's Insight):**
    The optimal solution to the KL-constrained reward maximization objective takes the form:

$$
\pi_{r}(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp \left( \frac{1}{\beta} r(x, y) \right)
$$

    where $Z(x) = \sum_{y} \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x) \exp \left( \frac{1}{\beta} r(x, y) \right)$ is the partition function.
*   **Reward Function Reparameterization (DPO):**
    Rearranging the optimal policy equation, the reward function can be expressed in terms of its optimal policy $\pi_r$ and reference policy $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$:

$$
r(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi_{r}(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)
$$

*   **Human Preference Probability in terms of Optimal Policy (DPO):**
    Substituting the reparameterized reward into the Bradley-Terry model, the partition function cancels, yielding:

$$
p^{*}(y_{1} \succ y_{2} \mid x) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp \left( \beta \log \frac{\pi^{*}(y_{2} \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_{2} \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi^{*}(y_{1} \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_{1} \mid x)} \right)}
$$

*   **DPO Loss Function:**
    The maximum likelihood objective for a parameterized policy $\pi_{\theta}$ is:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{DPO}}(\pi_{\theta}; \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x, y_{w}, y_{l}) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \log \sigma \left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_{w} \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_{w} \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_{\theta}(y_{l} \mid x)}{\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(y_{l} \mid x)} \right) \right]
$$

*   **Gradient of DPO Loss:**

$$
\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\pi_\theta; \pi_{\text{ref}}) = - \beta \mathbb{E}_{(x, y_w, y_l) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sigma(\hat{r}_\theta(x, y_l) - \hat{r}_\theta(x, y_w)) \left[ \nabla_\theta \log \pi(y_w \mid x) - \nabla_\theta \log \pi(y_l \mid x) \right] \right]
$$

    where $\hat{r}_\theta(x, y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)}$ is the implicitly defined reward.

**Key Quantitative Results and Numbers:**

*   **Sentiment Generation:** DPO produced the "most efficient frontier" in reward-KL trade-off, strictly dominating PPO and even PPO-GT (PPO with ground truth rewards).
*   **Summarization (Reddit TL;DR):**
    *   DPO achieved a win rate of approximately **61%** (at temperature 0.0) against reference summaries, exceeding PPO's best performance of **57%** (at its optimal sampling temperature of 0.0).
    *   DPO showed greater robustness to sampling temperature changes compared to PPO.
    *   In human evaluations, DPO samples (temp 0.25) were preferred **58%** of the time over PPO samples (temp 0).
*   **Single-Turn Dialogue (Anthropic Helpful and Harmless):**
    *   DPO was the only computationally efficient method that improved over the preferred completions in the Anthropic HH dataset.
    *   DPO provided similar or better performance to the computationally demanding "Best of 128" baseline.
*   **Generalization (CNN/DailyMail summarization):** DPO policies outperformed PPO policies by a significant margin on out-of-distribution news articles (DPO win rate 0.36 vs PPO win rate 0.26 at Temp 0).
*   **GPT-4 Evaluation Validation:** GPT-4 judgments correlated strongly with human judgments. For the "GPT-4 (C)" prompt, agreement with humans was similar or higher than inter-human annotator agreement (e.g., DPO: GPT-4(C)-H agree 67%, H-H agree 65%).

**Stated Limitations:**

*   **Generalization out of distribution:** While initial results suggest DPO policies generalize similarly to PPO, more comprehensive study is needed, especially regarding the use of unlabeled prompts.
*   **Reward over-optimization:** The manifestation of reward over-optimization in DPO and whether observed slight performance decreases are instances of it require further investigation.
*   **Scaling:** Experiments were conducted with models up to 6B parameters; scaling DPO to state-of-the-art models orders of magnitude larger is an exciting future direction.
*   **GPT-4 evaluation sensitivity:** The win rates computed by GPT-4 are impacted by the prompt used, suggesting a need for future work on eliciting high-quality judgments from automated systems.
*   **Applications beyond LMs:** Many possible applications of DPO exist beyond training LMs from human preferences, including generative models in other modalities.
