The study investigates the relaxation dynamics of shear-driven jamming in disordered elastic systems at zero temperature. The core problem is characterizing how the relaxation time $\tau_1$—extracted from post-shearing energy decay—correlates with initial configuration properties, specifically pressure $p_1$, and establishing optimal protocols for spacing shear strain increments $\gamma$ between configurations to efficiently compute critical exponents. The authors employ a two-step simulation protocol on a two-dimensional system of bi-disperse frictionless disks (radius ratio 1.4) under Lees-Edwards boundary conditions. First, the system is subjected to steady shear at a fixed rate $\dot{\gamma}$ to generate configurations at progressively increasing shear strain $\gamma$. Second, shearing is halted ($\dot{\gamma}=0$), and the system is allowed to relax until the energy per particle falls below $E < 10^{-20}$. The relaxation time $\tau_1$ is extracted by fitting the final exponential decay regime ($E(t) < 10^{-17}$) using $E(t) \sim e^{-t/\tau_1}$. Autocorrelation functions for $1/\tau_1$ and $p_1$ are computed as $\rho_A(\gamma) = \langle \delta A(\gamma' + \gamma) \delta A(\gamma') \rangle / \langle (\delta A)^2 \rangle$ to identify correlation shears $\gamma_\tau$ and $\gamma_p$. The ratio $f_1 = \ln(\tau_1/\eta_{p1})$, where $\eta_{p1} = p_1/\dot{\gamma}$, is analyzed to decouple pressure-dependent trends from relaxation-path fluctuations.

Key scaling relations govern the system near the jamming density $\phi_J$. The shear viscosity diverges as $\eta_{\mathrm{hd}}(\phi) \sim (\phi_J - \phi)^{-\beta}$, while the relaxation time follows $\tau(\phi, \dot{\gamma}) = A_\tau (\phi_J - \phi)^{-\beta}$ in the $\dot{\gamma} \to 0$ limit. Pressure scaling is described by $p(\phi, \dot{\gamma}) = \dot{\gamma}^q g((\phi - \phi_J)/\dot{\gamma}^{1/z\nu})$. The dissipation time, calculable directly from shearing data without relaxation, is given by $\tau_{\mathrm{diss}} = 2E/(\sigma \dot{\gamma})$. The velocity per unit shear rate scales as $v(\phi, \dot{\gamma})/\dot{\gamma} \sim A_v (\phi_J - \phi)^{-u_v}$ with $u_v \approx 1.1$.

Quantitative results from simulations with $N=4096$ and $16384$ particles at $\dot{\gamma}=10^{-8}$ and $\phi \in [0.830, 0.841]$ reveal that $\gamma_\tau$ decreases linearly as $\phi_J$ is approached, approximated by $\gamma_\tau \approx 3.25(\phi_J - \phi)$. In contrast, $\gamma_p$ exhibits non-monotonic behavior, decreasing to a minimum before rising near $\phi_J$, a trend attributed to the crossover between velocity-dominated dynamics ($t_v = \ell/v$) and relaxation-dominated dynamics ($\tau$). The spread in $f_1$ scales as $\mathrm{sdev}[f_1] \sim \sqrt{s}/\sqrt{N}$, where $s$ is the average particle displacement during relaxation. A fit yields $\sqrt{N} \cdot \mathrm{sdev}[f_1] \sim s^{0.49}$, indicating that each incremental displacement $\Delta s$ contributes a random multiplicative factor to $\tau_1$. Computational benchmarks indicate that $10^6$ time units require approximately two hours on 28 cores, with strain advancement of $2\gamma_\tau$ at $(\phi, \dot{\gamma}) = (0.842, 10^{-9})$ requiring roughly twenty hours.

The study identifies several limitations. Extracting precise $\gamma_\tau$ across multiple shear rates is hindered by statistical noise due to the computational expense of generating large $\tau_1$ ensembles. While finite-size effects manifest as $\tau \sim \ln N$ for randomly initialized systems, they are negligible for sheared initial conditions at $N \ge 4096$. However, double-precision arithmetic may introduce coordinate precision artifacts in very large systems due to fractional bit loss. Additionally, the authors note that logarithmic corrections to scaling remain debated, and fitting procedures for critical exponents can be sensitive to unconstrained parameters, potentially affecting the precision of $\beta/u_z$ determinations. The approximative nature of linking $\tau$ to pressure correlations under steady shear further constrains direct predictive modeling.
