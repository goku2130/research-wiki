**Core Problem**
The paper investigates polarization-dependent phenomena of linearized gravitational waves (GWs), specifically examining how the helicity of GWs couples to the rotational frame of an observer and to the gravitomagnetic field generated by rotating astronomical sources. The objective is to derive measurable deviations from standard relativistic kinematics (Doppler shift and aberration) and propagation effects (Faraday rotation and time delay), and to evaluate their observational feasibility with existing and next-generation GW detectors.

**Methodology**
The analysis is conducted within the linearized general relativity framework on a Minkowski background. First, GW polarization is formalized using transverse-traceless gauge matrices, transitioning from independent linear states ($\oplus, \otimes$) to circular helicity amplitudes $C_\pm$. Second, radiation reception by accelerated observers is modeled via an orthonormal tetrad formalism, projecting the Riemann curvature tensor to extract gravitoelectric and gravitomagnetic field components. Third, the gravitational Larmor theorem is invoked to map the observer's angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ to the local gravitomagnetic field $\mathbf{B}_g$. Fourth, a modified dispersion relation is derived in the rotating frame and extended to the exterior field of a rotating mass. Finally, Hamilton's equations are solved in the WKB limit to determine ray trajectories, phase velocities, and polarization evolution along unperturbed null paths.

**Key Formulas**
The helicity-rotation coupling modifies the observed frequency as $\omega' = \omega - \mathbf{H} \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}$, where $\mathbf{H} = \pm s \hat{\mathbf{k}}$ is the helicity vector ($s=2$ for gravitons), yielding $\omega' = \omega \mp 2\Omega$ for axial incidence. The gravitomagnetic vector potential for a slowly rotating source with angular momentum $\mathbf{J}$ is $\mathbf{A}_g(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{G}{c} \frac{\mathbf{J} \times \mathbf{x}}{|\mathbf{x}|^3}$. Incorporating the Larmor equivalence, the dispersion relation becomes $\omega^2 = c^2 k^2 \pm 2s\mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{B}_g$. The gravitational Faraday rotation angle accumulated along a ray is $\Theta = -\frac{s}{c^2}[\Psi_g(\bar{\mathbf{x}}) - \Psi_g(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i)]$, where $\Psi_g$ is the gravitomagnetic scalar potential. The polarization-dependent arrival time delay is given by $\Delta_P = -2\frac{sG}{c^3\omega}\frac{\mathbf{J}\cdot\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i}{|\bar{\mathbf{x}}_i|^3}$.

**Quantitative Results**
For Earth-based detectors observing $\nu \approx 100$ Hz GWs, the relative helicity-rotation frequency shift is $2\Omega_\oplus/\omega \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-7}$, roughly an order of magnitude smaller than the standard Doppler shift induced by Earth's rotation ($R_\oplus\Omega_\oplus/c \approx 1.5 \times 10^{-6}$). For gravitational lensing by an extreme Kerr black hole ($M \sim 10^6 M_\odot$), the gravitomagnetic time delay is $|\Delta_{gm}| \approx 10^{-3}$ s, which falls below current gamma-ray burst sampling rates of $\sim 10^{-1}$ s. The polarization time delay for binary black hole mergers emitting at $\nu \sim 100$ Hz is estimated at $|\Delta_P| \sim 1$ ms, with a broader range of $16$ $\mu$s to $16$ ms across the LIGO-Virgo sensitivity band (10 Hz–10 kHz). This delay scales linearly with the final black hole mass.

**Stated Limitations**
The derivations rely on the linear perturbation approximation and assume unperturbed straight null trajectories, explicitly neglecting ray bending. The spin-rotation coupling term $-\mathbf{H} \cdot \boldsymbol{\Omega}$ violates the hypothesis of locality, though it vanishes in the strict eikonal limit ($\omega \to \infty$). The calculated gravitomagnetic time delays for cosmological lensing events are currently undetectable due to instrumental sampling constraints. Furthermore, the helicity-rotation effects are inherently small; disentangling them from standard Doppler shifts requires networks of at least three detectors at fixed coordinates, or observations of continuous waves (e.g., pulsars) to leverage Earth's rotation over extended integration times.
