The paper addresses the scalability bottleneck of Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) and “zero” reasoning paradigms, which, despite eliminating supervised fine-tuning, still depend on manually curated question–answer pairs. This reliance on human-defined task distributions limits long-term autonomy and becomes increasingly unsustainable as AI systems approach or surpass human intelligence. To resolve this, the authors propose the **Absolute Zero** paradigm, where a single model autonomously generates, solves, and learns from its own tasks without any external data.

The **Absolute Zero Reasoner (AZR)** operationalizes this paradigm through a self-play loop using a Python code executor as a grounded, verifiable environment. The unified model assumes two roles: a proposer and a solver. During training, the proposer generates code-based reasoning tasks conditioned on past examples and one of three reasoning modes: deduction (predicting output given program and input), abduction (inferring input given program and output), or induction (synthesizing a program from input-output pairs and a description). Proposed tasks are validated for syntax, safety, and determinism before being added to task-specific buffers. The solver then attempts to solve the proposed tasks. Learning is guided by two rewards: a learnability reward for the proposer, which incentivizes tasks of moderate difficulty, and a binary correctness reward for the solver. A composite reward structure penalizes formatting errors. The policy is updated end-to-end using Task-Relative REINFORCE++ (TRR++), which computes normalized advantages separately for each of the six task-role configurations to reduce variance in this multitask setting.

The mathematical formulation begins with the standard supervised fine-tuning loss $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SFT}}(\theta) = - \mathbb{E}_{(x, c^*, y^*) \sim \mathcal{D}} \log \pi_\theta \left( c^*, y^* \mid x \right)$ and the RLVR objective $J_{\mathrm{RLVR}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x, y^*) \sim \mathcal{D}, (c, y) \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid x)} \left[ r(y, y^*) \right]$. Absolute Zero generalizes this to $\mathcal {J} (\theta) := \max _ {\theta} \mathbb {E} _ {z \sim p (z)} \left[ \mathbb {E} _ {(x, y ^ {*}) \sim f _ {e} (\cdot | \tau), \tau \sim \pi_ {\theta} ^ {\text {propose}} (\cdot | z)} \left[ \lambda r _ {e} ^ {\text {propose}} (\tau , \pi_ {\theta}) + \mathbb {E} _ {y \sim \pi_ {\theta} ^ {\text {solve}} (\cdot | x)} \left[ r _ {e} ^ {\text {solve}} (y, y ^ {*}) \right] \right] \right]$. The proposer reward is defined as $r _ {\text { propose }} = \left\{ \begin{array}{l l} 0, & \text { if } \bar {r} _ {\text { solve }} = 0 \\ 1 - \bar {r} _ {\text { solve }}, & \text { otherwise. } \end{array} \right.$, while the solver reward is $r _ {\text { solve }} = \mathbb {I} _ {(y = y ^ {*})}$. The composite reward applies penalties for formatting deviations, and TRR++ normalizes advantages via $A_{\text{task,role}}^{\text{norm}} = \frac{r - \mu_{\text{task,role}}}{\sigma_{\text{task,role}}}$.

Quantitatively, AZR-Coder-7B achieves state-of-the-art performance across coding and mathematical reasoning benchmarks despite training on zero external data. It surpasses models trained on tens of thousands of human-curated examples by an average of 1.8 absolute points in combined reasoning scores. The model exhibits strong cross-domain generalization: AZR-Base-7B and AZR-Coder-7B improve math accuracy by 10.9 and 15.2 points respectively, compared to a mere 0.65-point average gain for expert-trained code models. Performance scales with model capacity, yielding overall gains of +5.7, +10.2, and +13.2 points for 3B, 7B, and 14B coder variants. Ablation studies confirm that all three reasoning modes are essential; removing induction significantly degrades math performance, while conditioning the proposer on $K$ historical references and jointly training both roles yields the highest gains.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. The framework currently restricts task generation to deterministic programs to ensure verifiable rewards, explicitly excluding stochastic programs despite their potential. Safety concerns also emerge, as the Llama3.1-8B variant occasionally produces concerning chains of thought, termed “uh-oh moments,” highlighting the need for safety-aware training protocols. Furthermore, while joint proposer-solver training is beneficial, the authors note that proposer optimization could be enhanced through explicit diversity incentives and task-interference mitigation. Finally, the scaling laws governing the Absolute Zero paradigm remain unexplored, presenting a direction for future research.
