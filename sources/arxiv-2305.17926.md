The source identifies a critical vulnerability in the LLM-as-evaluator paradigm: severe positional bias. When large language models like GPT-4 and ChatGPT are prompted to score or compare candidate responses, their judgments are heavily skewed by the order in which responses appear. Swapping the presentation order frequently reverses evaluation outcomes, allowing model rankings to be manipulated without altering response content. This bias is quantified via a Conflict Rate, measuring the frequency of contradictory judgments when positions are inverted. GPT-4 exhibits a conflict rate of 46.3% when comparing Vicuna-13B and ChatGPT, while ChatGPT shows a substantially higher rate of 82.5%. Win rates for identical models fluctuate drastically based on position; for instance, Vicuna-13B’s win rate against ChatGPT drops from 51.3% to 23.8% under GPT-4 evaluation when moved from the first to the second position.

To mitigate this bias, the authors propose a three-stage calibration framework. First, Multiple Evidence Calibration (MEC) restructures the evaluation prompt to require the LLM to generate explanatory evidence before assigning numerical scores. This leverages the causal nature of autoregressive models to ground ratings in reasoning. The model is queried $k$ times to sample multiple evidence-score pairs, which are then averaged. Second, Balanced Position Calibration (BPC) eliminates positional preference by running MEC twice per example: once with the original response order and once with the responses swapped. The final calibrated score is computed by averaging the $2k$ results across both positions. Third, Human-in-the-Loop Calibration (HITLC) introduces a selective human annotation step. Using the evaluation outcomes from MEC and BPC, the framework calculates a Balanced Position Diversity Entropy (BPDE) score to quantify judgment uncertainty. Examples with the highest BPDE scores are flagged as high-risk for positional bias and are selected for human review. A threshold hyperparameter $\beta$ determines the proportion of examples requiring human intervention, after which human judgments are integrated via majority voting.

The mathematical formalization of the framework centers on three key equations. The Conflict Rate is defined as $\text{Conflict Rate} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}(\mathbf{ER}_i^{r12} \neq \mathbf{ER}_i^{r21})}{N}$, where $\mathbb{I}$ is an indicator function and $\mathbf{ER}$ denotes the evaluation result. The BPC calibrated score is calculated as $CS_R = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{S_R^i + S_R^{\prime i}}{2k}$ for $R \in \{r1, r2\}$, where $S_R^i$ and $S_R^{\prime i}$ represent scores at the first and second positions, respectively. Uncertainty is measured via BPDE: $\mathrm{BPDE} = \sum_{\mathbf{er} \in \{\text{win}, \text{tie}, \text{lose}\}} - \mathbf{p}_{\mathbf{er}} \log \mathbf{p}_{\mathbf{er}}$, with $\mathbf{p}_{\mathbf{er}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathbb{I}(\mathbf{ER}_i = \mathbf{er}) + \mathbb{I}(\mathbf{ER}_i^{\prime} = \mathbf{er})}{2k}$.

Quantitative experiments on 80 manually annotated Vicuna Benchmark queries demonstrate the framework’s efficacy. Against a human average accuracy of 71.7%, vanilla GPT-4 achieved 52.7%, which improved to 62.5% with MEC+BPC and reached 73.8% when combined with HITLC at $\beta=20\%$. ChatGPT’s accuracy improved by 14.3% (from 44.4% to 58.7%) using MEC+BPC, and matched human performance at 71.3% with HITLC. The HITLC strategy reduced annotation costs by 39% (from $30 to $18.3) while maintaining alignment with human judgments. Optimal performance was observed at $k=3$ with a sampling temperature between 0.6 and 1.0.

The study acknowledges several limitations. Positional bias is most pronounced when the quality gap between responses is small (score difference $\leq 1$), whereas large gaps ($\geq 3$) naturally suppress bias. The HITLC approach requires careful tuning of the $\beta$ threshold and still depends on API costs for the non-annotated subset. Additionally, the cost analysis assumes a fixed annotation time of three minutes per example and U.S. minimum wage rates, which may not generalize across regions or task complexities. Finally, while the calibration methods were validated on both scoring and pairwise comparison templates, the framework’s robustness across diverse evaluation paradigms and languages remains an open area for future investigation.
