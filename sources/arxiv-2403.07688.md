The paper challenges the conventional view that dying or saturated neurons degrade neural network performance and plasticity. Instead, it reframes neuron saturation as a controllable resource for efficient, dynamic structured pruning. The authors introduce Demon Pruning (DemP), a dense-to-sparse training methodology that actively promotes unit saturation and iteratively removes inactive neurons during optimization.

The DemP recipe integrates two primary mechanisms into the standard training loop. First, it applies a one-cycle regularization schedule exclusively to the scale parameters ($\gamma$) of normalization layers. This schedule features a linear warmup to a peak regularization strength $\lambda$, followed by a cosine decay to fine-tune sparsity levels while preserving accuracy. Second, it injects asymmetric Gaussian noise $\xi_{\mathrm{asym}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{t}^{2})$ solely into the weights of live neurons. The noise variance follows the identical one-cycle schedule as the regularization. Weights are updated using the composite gradient and noise term. Periodically every $\tau$ iterations (default $\tau=5000$), the algorithm identifies dead neurons—those consistently outputting zero (or negative values for non-ReLU activations) across a validation minibatch—and permanently removes them from the computation graph. This dynamic removal reduces embedding dimensions and accelerates training without disrupting the optimization trajectory.

Key mathematical formulations define the method's mechanics. The pre-activation vector after batch normalization is given by $\hat {\boldsymbol {z}} ^ {[ l ]} = \boldsymbol {\gamma} ^ {[ l ]} \odot \left(\frac {\mathbf {F} _ {l} (\mathbf {W} ^ {[ l ]} , \boldsymbol {a} ^ {[ l - 1 ]}) - \mu^ {[ l ]}}{\sqrt {\sigma^ {2 [ l ]} + \epsilon}}\right) + \boldsymbol {\beta} ^ {[ l ]}$. Sparsity is induced via Lasso regularization on scales, $R(\gamma;\lambda)=\lambda\|\gamma\|_{1}$. The weight update rule incorporates the loss gradient, regularization gradient, and asymmetric noise: $w_{t+1} \leftarrow w_t - \eta(\nabla L(w_t) + \nabla R(\gamma_t; \lambda_t)) + \xi_{\text{asym}}$. A neuron is formally defined as dead if its activation vector satisfies $a_j^\ell = 0$ across the dataset, with a practical threshold relaxation of $|a_i^\ell| < \epsilon$. The authors theoretically ground this behavior in multiplicative gradient noise, noting that regions with lower noise magnitude act as stochastic attractors, trapping neurons in the inactive domain.

Empirical evaluations on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet demonstrate DemP's efficacy. On ResNet-18 and VGG-16, DemP outperforms structured baselines like EarlyCrop and SNAP by up to 2.5% accuracy at 80% sparsity. Training speedups reach 2.45× on ResNet-18 and 3.56× on VGG-16. For ResNet-50 on ImageNet with the Adam optimizer, DemP achieves 71.52% test accuracy at ~80% weight sparsity, surpassing EarlySNAP (68.67%) and EarlyCroP-S (68.26%), while maintaining 66.34% accuracy at ~90% sparsity versus 63.80% and 64.20% respectively. In transformer architectures, pruning the MLP layers of a ViT-B/16 model yields 90% neuron sparsity with 71.38% accuracy on ImageNet; replacing GeLU with ReLU retains 77.62% accuracy with 43.7% neuron sparsity.

The authors acknowledge several limitations. DemP's performance with the SGDM optimizer degrades abruptly at extreme sparsities (~95% weight sparsity), which may stem from suboptimal tuning of the one-cycle scheduler for shorter training epochs. The method's efficacy is closely tied to the presence of normalization layers; without them, the approach defaults to traditional parameter regularization. Additionally, balancing accuracy and sparsity requires careful calibration of the peak regularization strength and noise variance, as excessive noise or regularization degrades performance. The pruning criterion relies on minibatch approximations rather than full-dataset evaluation, which may occasionally misclassify neurons that would otherwise revive, though empirical overlap analysis indicates minimal impact on final accuracy.
