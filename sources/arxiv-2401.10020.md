Standard LLM alignment via RLHF or DPO relies on frozen reward models or static human preference datasets, creating a performance bottleneck capped by human annotation quality and preventing the reward signal from evolving during training. The authors address this by introducing Self-Rewarding Language Models, which unify instruction generation and reward modeling into a single continuously updating system.

The approach employs an iterative Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) framework. Initialization begins with a base pretrained LLM ($M_0$), which undergoes Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) on two seed datasets: Instruction Fine-Tuning (IFT) data for general instruction following, and Evaluation Fine-Tuning (EFT) data containing LLM-as-a-Judge prompts with chain-of-thought justifications and additive 5-point scores. This yields the first iteration model $M_1$. Subsequent iterations follow a three-step self-alignment loop: (1) Self-Instruction Creation: A fixed external model generates novel prompts via few-shot prompting from the IFT seed set. The current model $M_t$ generates $N=4$ candidate responses per prompt. (2) Self-Evaluation: $M_t$ acts as its own reward model, scoring each candidate response $y_i^n$ using an additive five-criteria rubric (relevance, coverage, usefulness, clarity, expertise), yielding scores $r_i^n \in [0, 5]$. Scores are averaged across three sampled evaluations to mitigate variance. (3) Preference Optimization: Winning and losing responses are paired from the highest and lowest scores. Ties are discarded, and the resulting AI Feedback Training (AIFT) dataset is used to train $M_{t+1}$ via DPO. The iterative sequence is formally defined as $M_0 \to M_1 \text{ (SFT on IFT+EFT)} \to M_2 \text{ (DPO on AIFT}(M_1)\text{)} \to M_3 \text{ (DPO on AIFT}(M_2)\text{)}$.

Evaluated on Llama 2 70B, the method demonstrates simultaneous gains in instruction following and reward modeling. On the AlpacaEval 2.0 leaderboard (win rate against GPT-4 Turbo), performance scales from $M_1$ at 9.94% to $M_2$ at 15.38%, and $M_3$ at 20.44%, surpassing Claude 2 (17.19%), Gemini Pro (16.85%), and GPT-4 0613 (15.76%). Head-to-head evaluations confirm $M_2$ outperforms $M_1$ with a 55.5% win rate, while $M_3$ achieves 47.7% against $M_2$. MT-Bench scores similarly improve from 6.85 (SFT baseline) to 7.25 ($M_3$). Reward modeling alignment with human preferences also progresses: pairwise accuracy rises from 65.1% (SFT baseline) to 78.7% ($M_1$), 80.4% ($M_2$), and 81.7% ($M_3$). The generated preference datasets scale in size, with AIFT($M_1$) containing 3,964 pairs and AIFT($M_2$) containing 6,942 pairs. Notably, average response length increases across iterations (1,092 for $M_1$, 1,552 for $M_2$, 2,552 for $M_3$), correlating with quality gains.

The authors caution that results are preliminary, with only three iterations tested in a single configuration, leaving scaling laws and long-term saturation unexplored. The observed increase in generation length introduces potential reward-hacking vulnerabilities, as length correlates with estimated quality. Safety evaluations were not conducted, though the authors note that iterative reward modeling could theoretically enhance safety alignment over time. Additionally, the method exhibits an "alignment tax" on standard NLP benchmarks (e.g., ARC-Challenge, GSM8K), where performance slightly regresses compared to the base model, likely due to the narrow distribution of Open Assistant seed prompts. Finally, prompt generation initially relied on a fixed external model for computational efficiency, though self-generated prompts were shown to be feasible.
