Reinforcement learning (RL) for large language models (LLMs) is fundamentally constrained by data scarcity, which manifests in two complementary forms: limited high-cost external supervision (e.g., human feedback, preference data, expert annotations, step-by-step reasoning traces) and restricted internal experience (e.g., rollout budgets, trajectory lengths, exploration limits). As scaling compute or data yields diminishing returns, the field requires data-efficient RL paradigms that maximize information extraction from minimal signals.

To systematically address this bottleneck, the authors propose a bottom-up hierarchical framework organized across three complementary perspectives. The data-centric perspective optimizes the training pool through three sequential operations: (1) data pruning, which applies offline heuristics, online difficulty estimation, or fine-grained influence functions to retain high-information samples; (2) data synthesis, which expands distributional coverage via static generation, dynamic online annotators, or weakness-driven hard problem creation; and (3) data compression, which filters tokens, reasoning steps, trajectories, or entire datasets to preserve only non-zero gradient signals. The training-centric perspective improves policy updates under constrained budgets by implementing guided exploration (e.g., Monte Carlo Tree Search integration, Bayesian posterior sampling), selective rollouts (pre- and post-generation filtering), advanced reward engineering (process-based rewards, intrinsic motivation via entropy minimization/maximization, consistency/consensus mechanisms, and heuristic surrogates), and sample-efficient policy optimization (experience replay adaptations and distillation mechanisms). The framework-centric perspective constructs autonomous learning loops through self-evolving single-model systems, asymmetric co-evolution (collaborative proposer-solver or adversarial generator-discriminator pairs), and multi-agent evolution (competitive self-play games and multi-role cooperative structures). This structured recipe shifts reliance from static external datasets to dynamic, experience-driven optimization.

The source does not provide explicit mathematical equations for policy updates but references key mathematical principles and empirical metrics. Conceptually, reasoning is modeled as a sequence $q \rightarrow r \rightarrow a$ (question-reason-answer), and multi-objective reward scalarization is framed as a convex optimization achieving Pareto convergence at $O(n)$ complexity. Quantitatively, the field exhibits explosive growth, with surveyed publications rising from 1 in 2018/2020 to 109 by December 2025. Specific data-efficiency benchmarks include: One-shot RLVR achieving performance comparable to 7.5k-sample baselines using a single training example; BAL-PM reducing required human labels by 33–68%; ADPO halving query complexity while matching DPO performance; and UFO-RL matching full-data performance using only 10% of the training set.

The survey acknowledges its own limitation: the rapid pace of development necessitates timely updates to maintain comprehensive coverage of emerging methods. Regarding the technical landscape, the authors identify three critical challenges. First, internal reward signals (consistency, entropy, heuristics) are often noisy and vulnerable to reward hacking or model collapse, complicating reliable credit assignment. Second, current methods heavily favor verifiable domains (mathematics, coding), leaving unverifiable, subjective, or open-ended tasks (e.g., creative writing, embodied agents) underexplored. Third, self-play and autonomous evolution frameworks introduce safety risks, including bias amplification from initial seeds, spurious generalization patterns, and potential reasoning failures, requiring robust online filtering and safety-aware training mechanisms.
