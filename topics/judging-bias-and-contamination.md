LLM-as-a-judge evaluation paradigms suffer from systematic biases including self-preference, where models favor their own outputs, and preference leakage, where judges favor students trained on related generators' data. These biases conflate stylistic familiarity with quality and threaten the validity of automated alignment benchmarks.

## Self-Preference Bias: Mechanisms and Measurement

### Definitions and Formal Metrics
Self-preference bias is the tendency of an LLM evaluator to assign higher scores to its own generations than to those of other models or humans, even when quality is equivalent. [source:arxiv:2410.21819] formalizes this using algorithmic fairness metrics. Treating the evaluator $f$ as a classifier, they define bias via **Equal Opportunity**:

$$
\text{Bias}_{\text{EO}} = P(Y' = 1 \mid S = 1, Y = 1) - P(Y' = 1 \mid S = 0, Y = 1)
$$

where $Y$ is the human-preferred response index, $Y'$ is the $f$-preferred index, and $S$ indicates whether the response was generated by $f$. They also compute a **Demographic Parity** metric:

$$
\text{Bias}_{\text{DP}} = P(Y' = 1 \mid S = 1) - P(Y' = 1 \mid S = 0)
$$

which measures the unconditional preference for self-generated outputs. [source:neurips:llm-evaluators-recognize-and-favor-their] measures pairwise preference confidence from output token probabilities:

$$
\text{Confidence} = \frac{P(\text{option}_A)}{P(\text{option}_A) + P(\text{option}_B)}
$$

and individual Likert ratings as $\sum_{i=1}^5 i \cdot P(\text{token}_i)$.

### The Familiarity vs. Recognition Debate
**Source [source:arxiv:2410.21819] argues self-preference is a "familiarity preference":** LLMs assign higher evaluations to lower-perplexity text regardless of authorship. Because models naturally produce low-perplexity text relative to their own distribution, they favor their own outputs incidentally. They find GPT-4 prefers low-perplexity responses even when not self-generated, and the correlation holds across most models (except dolly-v2-12b and stablelm-tuned-alpha-7b). **Source [source:neurips:llm-evaluators-recognize-and-favor-their] contradicts this, claiming self-recognition causes self-preference.** They demonstrate a linear correlation between an LLM's self-recognition accuracy and its self-preference strength. Fine-tuning GPT-3.5 and Llama-2 on 500 examples to boost recognition accuracy above 90% correspondingly increased self-preference. They also show models distinguish self from humans most easily, but Llama-2 cannot distinguish itself from GPT-3.5/4. **Source [source:aclanthology:beyond-the-surface-measuring-self-prefer] adds a third dimension: quality confounding.** They argue naive win-rate differences conflate genuine quality advantages with bias. Their **DBG score** isolates bias using gold judgments $w^*$ from an ensemble of strong models (GPT-4o-mini, Gemini-1.5-Flash, DeepSeek-V3):

$$
\hat{w}_A = w_A - w^* = \mathbb{E}_x[\sigma(\delta + b_A)] - \mathbb{E}_x[\sigma(\delta)]
$$

where $\delta = Q(r_A)-Q(r_B)$ is the quality gap and $b_A$ is inherent bias. This reveals that larger models have dramatically less bias: Llama-3.1-8B shows 21.6% DBG vs. 0.4% for Llama-3.1-70B; Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct shows 41.7% vs. 2.1% for Qwen2.5-14B-Instruct. Reasoning models (DS-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B, 4.8%) exhibit higher bias than non-reasoning counterparts (Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct, 2.6%).

## Preference Leakage: Cross-Model Contamination

### Distinct from Egocentric Bias
[source:arxiv:2502.01534] identifies **preference leakage** as a contamination problem where the judge $M_J$ favors a student $M_S$ trained on synthetic data from a related generator $M_G$. Unlike egocentric bias (self-preference), leakage occurs because $M_S$ inherits "spurious features"—style, format, wording—from $M_G$, which $M_J$ is predisposed to favor. They define three relatedness tiers:
1. **Same Model**: $M_G \equiv M_J$
2. **Inheritance**: One fine-tuned from the other or trained on the other's outputs
3. **Same Family**: Shared architecture and overlapping pre-training data (e.g., GPT versions)

### Preference Leakage Score (PLS)
For a model pair $(i,j)$:

$$
\mathrm{PLS}(i,j) = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\mathrm{WR}(i,i) - \mathrm{AVG}(i,j)}{\mathrm{AVG}(i,j)} + \frac{\mathrm{WR}(j,j) - \mathrm{AVG}(j,i)}{\mathrm{AVG}(j,i)}\right)
$$

where $\mathrm{WR}(i,j)$ is the win-rate of student $i$ judged by judge $j$, and $\mathrm{AVG}(i,j) = (\mathrm{WR}(i,i) + \mathrm{WR}(i,j))/2$. The leakage condition is:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{x,y_S\sim P_{M_S}}[S_{M_J}(y_S|x) \mid M_G \sim_{\text{rel}} M_J] > \mathbb{E}_{x,y_S\sim P_{M_S}}[S_{M_J'}(y_S|x) \mid M_G \not\sim_{\text{rel}} M_J']
$$

### Key Findings
- **Prevalence**: Mistral-7B and Qwen-2.5-14B show average PLS of 23.6% and 27.9% with GPT-4o/Gemini-1.5 generator/judge pairs.
- **Relatedness gradient**: Same Model (23.6%) > Inheritance (19.3–22.3%) > Same Family/Series (8.9%).
- **Training method**: SFT exhibits highest leakage (23.6%); DPO significantly lower (5.2%); ICL shows negligible/negative leakage (-2.7%).
- **Model size**: Smaller students (Llama-3-1B, Qwen-2.5-3B) exhibit higher PLS.
- **Detectability**: Judges fail to recognize their students (near random), but a BERT classifier achieves 82.4% accuracy distinguishing student responses.
- **Mitigation**: Contextual calibration reduces Error Bias from 17.8 (base) to 7.3; paraphrasing, CoT, and prompting are less effective.
- **Subjectivity**: Leakage is more pronounced in subjective tasks (writing, programming) and subjective dimensions (fairness) vs. objective ones (mathematics, completeness).

## Evaluation Biases: Position, Verbosity, and Reasoning

[source:arxiv:2306.05685] systematically documents three pervasive biases in LLM judges:
- **Position bias**: Zero-shot GPT-4 consistency 65.0%; few-shot improves to 77.5%. Mitigated by swapping response order and requiring consistent preference.
- **Verbosity bias**: Under a "repetitive list" attack, Claude-v1 and GPT-3.5 fail 91.3% of the time; GPT-4 is significantly more resilient.
- **Reasoning failures**: On math grading (e.g., $|x+5|<10$), GPT-4 default failure rate 14/20 (marking incorrect as correct); CoT reduces to 6/20; reference-guided to 3/20.

[source:aclanthology:beyond-the-surface-measuring-self-prefer] finds rewriting responses into a unified "attractive" or "humorous" style reduces Llama-3.1-8B DBG from 18.7% to 7.2% and 5.9% respectively, indicating style uniformity mitigates bias.

## Data Contamination in Evaluation

[source:arxiv:2305.15717] warns that evaluation benchmarks (MMLU, NQ, HumanEval) may be contaminated in the proprietary teacher's (ChatGPT) pre-training data, inflating its scores and distorting imitation studies. They note this as a confounder when comparing imitation models to ChatGPT. [source:arxiv:2502.01534] acknowledges few student-teacher pairs are publicly documented in leaderboards, limiting real-world leakage analysis. ## Current Status and Trajectory

LLM-as-a-judge is the **default evaluation paradigm** for alignment leaderboards (Chatbot Arena, MT-bench, AlpacaEval, Arena-Hard) [source:arxiv:2306.05685][source:arxiv:2502.01534], but the field is in a **transition from naive usage to bias-aware engineering**. Self-preference measurement has evolved from simple win-rate gaps [source:arxiv:2410.21819] to recognition-aware causal analysis [source:neurips:llm-evaluators-recognize-and-favor-their] and quality-deconfounded metrics (DBG) [source:aclanthology:beyond-the-surface-measuring-self-prefer]. Preference leakage is a **newly identified, rapidly rising concern** (2025) [source:arxiv:2502.01534] with immediate implications for distillation pipelines and leaderboard integrity. Mitigations are fragmented: positional debiasing and reference-guided grading are standard practice [source:arxiv:2306.05685]; contextual calibration and style unification show promise but are not yet widely adopted; DPO's lower leakage vs. SFT [source:arxiv:2502.01534] may shift distillation preferences. No unified bias-mitigation framework exists; the trajectory points toward **evaluation suites that bundle multiple calibrations** (position, style, reference, contextual) and **gold-judge ensembles** for bias quantification. The field has not abandoned LLM judges—human agreement remains high (85%) [source:arxiv:2306.05685]—but treats them as noisy instruments requiring careful calibration.

## Key Takeaways
- **Self-preference is multi-faceted**: familiarity/perplexity-driven [source:arxiv:2410.21819], recognition-driven [source:neurips:llm-evaluators-recognize-and-favor-their], and quality-confounded [source:aclanthology:beyond-the-surface-measuring-self-prefer]. Larger models exhibit dramatically less bias (Llama-3.1-70B 0.4% DBG vs. 8B 21.6%).
- **Preference leakage is distinct from self-preference**: it arises from generator–judge relatedness contaminating student evaluation, with PLS up to 27.9% for SFT students [source:arxiv:2502.01534]. DPO and ICL substantially reduce it.
- **Judges fail to detect their own biases**: LLMs cannot recognize their students (near random) [source:arxiv:2502.01534] and show systematic positional, verbosity, and reasoning failures [source:arxiv:2306.05685].
- **Mitigation requires layered defenses**: position swapping, CoT, reference-guided grading, style unification, shared training data, and contextual calibration each address different bias vectors. No single fix suffices.
- **Data contamination remains under-quantified**: benchmark leakage in teacher models [source:arxiv:2305.15717] and limited public student-teacher records [source:arxiv:2502.01534] hinder real-world impact assessment.
